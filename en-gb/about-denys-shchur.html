About the Author — Denys Shchur

Last updated: 25 November 2025

I’m Denys Shchur, the person behind the content on VPN World. My work sits at the intersection of VPN testing, cybersecurity and search — I write long-form guides that are meant to be both technically sound and easy to follow.

VPN World was born as a side project that gradually turned into a structured, multi-language knowledge base. A lot of my time goes into checking how VPNs behave in realistic UK and European scenarios: streaming BBC iPlayer or DAZN, dealing with geo-blocked services, working from home on shared connections, or joining public Wi-Fi on the go. The goal is simple: explain what actually works, not just what looks good in marketing copy.

In parallel to VPN World, I run SmartAdvisorOnline — an English-only project focused on VPN advice, security best practices and structured how-to guides around remote work, online privacy and “less noise, more signal” tech choices.

How I approach writing on VPN World

When I start a new guide, I usually look at three angles:

  • Real usage — how a tool behaves when you actually connect from a flat, a café, a co-working space or a hotel Wi-Fi in a different country.
  • Technical underpinnings — protocols, encryption, DNS handling, kill switches, split tunnelling and how they’re implemented.
  • Long-term perspective — what happens when you use the same VPN every day for months, not just for a quick screenshot.

I try to keep the language direct and practical: instead of repeating vendor slogans, I’d rather answer questions such as “Will this unblock the match I want to watch?”, “Is this safe enough for online banking?” or “Will this slow down my home Wi-Fi too much?”.

Security, privacy and the small details

Security on paper and security in the real world are not the same thing. From a user’s point of view, it matters whether:

  • your DNS requests are handled properly or quietly leaked to your ISP,
  • a VPN keeps any logs that could be matched to your traffic,
  • the provider uses RAM-only servers and up-to-date protocols,
  • a kill switch actually stops traffic when the tunnel drops.

That’s why many of my guides go deeper into topics such as geo-blocks, DNS behaviour, dedicated IPs, or two-factor authentication. The idea is not to overwhelm you with jargon, but to show which settings are worth changing and which you can safely ignore.

How testing works in practice

I try to build tests around everyday patterns rather than lab conditions. That includes:

  • streaming UK and international services (BBC iPlayer, TVP, Netflix, DAZN),
  • connecting from home broadband as well as congested evening Wi-Fi,
  • using VPNs on phones, laptops, smart TVs and consoles,
  • running traffic through public networks, airport Wi-Fi and hotspots,
  • combining VPN usage with banking apps, work tools and gaming.

Along the way I pay attention not only to speed tests, but also to “soft” factors: whether apps are stable, whether they cause friction on weaker devices, and whether the overall setup feels realistic for someone who is not a network engineer.

Transparency and affiliate links

VPN World uses affiliate links for some providers. If you choose to subscribe via one of those links, I may receive a commission at no additional cost to you. This helps fund new tests, tools and time spent updating older articles.

Recommendations are not pay-to-play. If a service performs poorly, that is reflected in the content. If a feature is more marketing than substance, I try to say so clearly and offer alternatives.
