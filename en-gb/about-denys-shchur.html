About the Author — Denys Shchur

Last updated: 25 November 2025

My name is Denys Shchur and I’m the person behind the content on VPN World. I test VPN services, streaming platforms and security tools, then translate the technical results into practical, real-world guides in Polish and several other languages.

VPN World started as an independent “SEO + security laboratory”. On one side, I check how VPNs and privacy tools behave in real conditions: on slower connections, in different countries, with popular VOD services and social platforms. On the other side, I make sure every guide is written so it can help both a technical reader and someone who simply wants their VPN to “just work and stay out of the way.”

How content on VPN World is created

Each major guide on VPN World is built on a mix of three elements:

  • real-world tests of speed, stability and compatibility with popular services,
  • knowledge of VPN protocols, encryption and network configuration,
  • experience from long-term SEO and content projects.

I don’t work exclusively with a single provider — in the articles you’ll find concrete scenarios: when a given VPN makes sense, when you should look for an alternative, or when the real solution is something else entirely (for example, changing router settings or enabling 2FA/MFA).

Why I care so much about technical details

VPNs, encryption, DNS, protocols, logs — it all sounds very technical. In practice, small details decide whether:

  • your connection is truly encrypted end-to-end,
  • websites and services see a “VPN tunnel” or just regular traffic,
  • a VOD platform blocks access after an IP change or lets you keep watching,
  • your account gets taken over after phishing or 2FA/MFA saves it.

That’s why you’ll frequently see terms such as WireGuard, NordLynx, RAM-only servers, DNS leaks or kill switch in my writing. I always try to explain what they mean in practice: whether you should worry about them if you “just want to use the internet more safely.”

How I test VPNs and security tools

When testing VPNs and other services, I try to recreate typical everyday scenarios:

  • watching VOD platforms (Netflix, DAZN, BBC iPlayer, local streaming services),
  • using public Wi-Fi in cafés, stations and airports,
  • remote access to work (corporate VPN, remote desktops, SSH),
  • daily browsing, cloud work and email,
  • online gaming, where ping and stability matter more than raw speed.

Instead of focusing only on synthetic benchmarks, I care more about answers to questions like: can you watch a match without buffering, does the connection drop at the worst possible moment, does the VPN app conflict with other software, and can you safely recommend this setup to your family.

Transparency and affiliate links

On VPN World I use affiliate links to selected VPN services. If you buy a subscription through one of these links, I may receive a commission — at no extra cost to you. This helps me keep the project running, test more tools and update existing guides.

However, which services I recommend positively does not depend on affiliate programs, but on real tests. If something performs poorly, I say so directly. If a feature is unnecessary for most users, I point that out as well.
