Quick answer: free VPNs are fine for occasional, low-risk tasks (public Wi-Fi, basic browsing), but they usually have data caps, fewer servers, slower links, weaker streaming and thinner support. For streaming, P2P, remote work or sustained privacy, a reputable paid plan is far more reliable.

When a free VPN is OK

  • Short sessions on café/airport Wi-Fi to cut hotspot snooping — pair with our public Wi-Fi safety checklist.
  • Occasional IP change for generic sites and trials.
  • Learning/testing clients and protocols — compare in VPN protocols.

Limits you’ll hit with free tiers

  • Speed & ping: congestion and distance matter — measure with the VPN speed test.
  • Servers/locations: fewer exits make it harder to find a good node; see which VPN server to choose.
  • Streaming: public free IPs are often blocked; read geo-blocks & VPN.
  • Privacy/trust: if there’s no subscription, monetisation may be ads/telemetry — choose transparent policies and (ideally) audits.

Safety checklist for any free VPN

  1. Run a DNS/IPv6 leak test (with Kill Switch toggled).
  2. Prefer WireGuard/OpenVPN; fall back to TCP on unstable networks — see protocols.
  3. Pick the closest, low-load location — tips in server choice.
  4. Know the rules — quick primer: Is a VPN legal?

When a paid VPN is the smarter move

For smooth streaming, frequent travel, P2P, remote work or everyday privacy, you’ll value consistent throughput, broader locations, 24/7 support and audited no-logs. Annual plans often cost pennies per day and save hours of tinkering.
