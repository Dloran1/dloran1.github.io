VPNs (Virtual Private Networks) are widely used to enhance online privacy by encrypting your internet traffic and masking your IP address. However, a DNS leak can undermine these protections by exposing your DNS queries to your internet service provider (ISP) or other third parties, even when connected to a VPN. Understanding how to detect and fix DNS leaks is essential for maintaining your online privacy in 2026.

While VPNs offer significant privacy benefits, they are not flawless. DNS leaks remain one of the common pitfalls that can compromise your anonymity. This guide will walk you through recognising when a DNS leak occurs, how to test for it, and practical steps to resolve it across various platforms.

Quick answer

  • A DNS leak happens when your device sends DNS queries outside the VPN tunnel.
  • It exposes your browsing activity to your ISP or network observer despite VPN use.
  • Testing tools like DNSLeakTest.com or IPLeak.net help detect leaks.
  • Fixes vary by platform but often include adjusting DNS settings, disabling IPv6, or enabling VPN kill switches.

When does a DNS leak happen?

Key takeaway: A DNS leak occurs when your device bypasses the VPN’s DNS servers and sends DNS requests directly to your ISP’s DNS servers or any other non-VPN DNS resolver. This can happen due to misconfigured VPN software, operating system defaults, or network settings that prioritise local DNS over VPN tunnels.

More specifically, DNS leaks happen if your VPN does not properly route DNS queries through its encrypted tunnel or if your device falls back to default DNS servers when the VPN connection is unstable or interrupted. For example, some VPN clients on Windows may fail to override the system DNS settings fully, causing queries to leak. IPv6 traffic is also a common culprit, as many VPNs do not handle IPv6 DNS requests effectively.

Limitations and edge cases

Key takeaway: Even with a VPN that claims no DNS leaks, certain network or device configurations can cause leaks. For instance, split tunnelling or using custom DNS resolvers outside the VPN can expose DNS requests. Additionally, some VPN providers might not support IPv6 or WebRTC leak protection, creating edge cases where leaks persist despite precautions.

Moreover, VPNs cannot guarantee complete anonymity, especially if other data points like cookies or browser fingerprinting are in play. It’s important to view DNS leak protection as one part of a broader privacy strategy rather than a silver bullet. For more on VPN limitations, see disadvantages of VPNs.

How to test DNS/IPv6/WebRTC leaks

Key takeaway: Testing for leaks requires checking DNS requests, IPv6 traffic, and WebRTC IP exposure. Different tools specialise in each area, and running multiple tests ensures comprehensive coverage.

Test Type Recommended Tool What it Checks
DNS Leak DNSLeakTest.com Detects if DNS queries are sent outside the VPN tunnel.
IPv6 Leak IPLeak.net Checks if IPv6 traffic bypasses the VPN, revealing your real IP.
WebRTC Leak IPLeak.net Tests if your browser’s WebRTC feature exposes your true IP address.

Running these tests while connected to your VPN is the best way to confirm whether your DNS or IP information is leaking. If you detect a leak, refer to the platform-specific fixes below.

Fixes by platform

Windows

  • Disable IPv6 in your network adapter settings to prevent IPv6 leaks.
  • Manually set your DNS servers to your VPN provider’s DNS or a trusted public DNS like Cloudflare (1.1.1.1) or Google (8.8.8.8).
  • Enable the VPN’s built-in kill switch and DNS leak protection features if available.
  • Flush DNS cache via Command Prompt: ipconfig /flushdns after changes.
  • Consider disabling Smart Multi-Homed Name Resolution in Group Policy Editor to avoid Windows sending DNS queries outside the VPN.

macOS

  • Use your VPN’s DNS servers by manually configuring DNS in System Preferences → Network → Advanced → DNS.
  • Disable IPv6 on the active network interface via Terminal with networksetup -setv6off Wi-Fi or Ethernet accordingly.
  • Ensure your VPN client supports DNS leak protection and is up to date.
  • Clear your DNS cache with sudo killall -HUP mDNSResponder.

Android

  • Use VPN apps that offer DNS leak protection and support IPv6 blocking.
  • Switch to a VPN provider known for strong DNS leak protections (see best free VPNs in the UK for some options).
  • Disable Private DNS (DNS over TLS) in Android settings if it conflicts with your VPN’s DNS handling.
  • Manually configure DNS in Wi-Fi settings if your VPN app does not override DNS queries properly.

iOS

  • Use VPN apps with built-in DNS leak protection and IPv6 leak prevention.
  • Disable IPv6 by configuring your router or using VPNs that block IPv6 traffic.
  • Manually set DNS servers in Wi-Fi settings if needed, but this can be cumbersome on iOS.
  • Keep your device and VPN app updated to benefit from security improvements.

Router

  • Configure your router to use your VPN provider’s DNS servers or trusted third-party DNS.
  • Disable IPv6 support on your router if your VPN does not handle it, preventing IPv6 leaks.
  • Use router firmware that supports VPN kill switches or DNS leak protection (e.g., DD-WRT, OpenWrt).
  • Check if your router supports DNS over HTTPS or DNS over TLS to encrypt DNS queries independently.

Troubleshooting checklist

  1. Confirm your VPN is connected and routing all traffic through its tunnel.
  2. Run DNS leak tests on DNSLeakTest.com and IPLeak.net.
  3. Disable IPv6 on your device and router if your VPN does not support it.
  4. Manually set DNS servers to your VPN provider’s or trusted public DNS.
  5. Enable DNS leak protection and kill switch features in your VPN app.
  6. Flush your DNS cache to clear any stored queries.
  7. Ensure your VPN app and device OS are fully updated.
  8. Test WebRTC leaks in your browser and disable WebRTC if necessary using browser extensions.
  9. Check if split tunnelling or custom network configurations are causing leaks and disable them temporarily.
  10. Restart your device and router to apply all changes properly.
  11. Consider switching VPN providers if leaks persist—some free VPNs often lack robust leak protection (see free vs paid VPNs).
  12. Review your firewall or antivirus settings to ensure they aren’t interfering with VPN traffic.

FAQ

What exactly is a DNS leak?

A DNS leak occurs when your DNS queries, which translate domain names into IP addresses, are sent outside the encrypted VPN tunnel—usually to your ISP’s DNS servers—exposing your browsing activity despite using a VPN.

Can a VPN leak my IP address?

Yes, certain leaks like IPv6 or WebRTC leaks can expose your real IP address if the VPN or device is not configured properly. Using leak protection features and testing regularly helps mitigate this risk.

Are free VPNs more likely to have DNS leaks?

Often, yes. Many free VPNs lack robust DNS leak protection or proper infrastructure, increasing the chance of leaks. For better security, consider reputable paid VPNs or well-reviewed free options discussed in our free vs paid VPN guide.

How does IPv6 cause DNS leaks?

Many VPNs only route IPv4 traffic through their tunnel, leaving IPv6 traffic to go directly through your ISP. This can leak DNS queries and your real IP if IPv6 is enabled but unsupported by the VPN.

What is WebRTC and why is it a privacy concern?

WebRTC is a browser feature that enables peer-to-peer connections. It can reveal your real IP address to websites even when you use a VPN unless you disable it or use browser extensions that block WebRTC leaks.

Is disabling IPv6 always recommended?

Disabling IPv6 is a common workaround to prevent leaks, but it depends on your VPN’s support. Some VPNs now handle IPv6 properly, so check your provider’s documentation before disabling it.

How often should I test for DNS leaks?

Regular testing is a good habit, especially after VPN updates or device changes. Testing before sensitive activities like online banking or streaming geo-blocked content can provide peace of mind.

Can routers cause DNS leaks?

Yes, if your router’s DNS settings or firmware do not properly route DNS queries through the VPN, leaks can occur. Configuring DNS on your router and disabling IPv6 can help. See our VPN for Smart TV guide for router advice related to VPN use.
