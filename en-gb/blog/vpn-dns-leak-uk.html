Using a VPN is a popular way to enhance online privacy and security, but it’s not without pitfalls. One of the lesser-known issues that can compromise your anonymity is a DNS leak. This occurs when your DNS queries bypass the VPN tunnel and are sent directly to your ISP’s DNS servers, potentially exposing your browsing activity despite using a VPN.

Understanding and addressing DNS leaks is crucial, especially as VPNs become more widely used for everything from streaming geo-blocked content to protecting sensitive data. This article explores what DNS leaks are, how to detect them, and practical fixes across different platforms to help you maintain better privacy in 2026.

Quick answer

  • A DNS leak happens when DNS requests bypass the VPN, revealing your true IP or location.
  • Common causes include misconfigured VPN clients, IPv6 traffic leaks, or OS-level DNS settings.
  • Testing for leaks involves specialised websites and tools that check DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC leaks.
  • Fixes vary by platform but often include manual DNS configuration, disabling IPv6, or using VPN apps with built-in leak protection.

When does a DNS leak happen?

DNS leaks typically occur when your device’s DNS queries are sent outside the encrypted VPN tunnel, often due to default network settings or VPN client limitations. For example, if a VPN only tunnels IPv4 traffic but your device uses IPv6, DNS requests over IPv6 can leak. Similarly, some VPNs fail to override the system’s DNS settings, leading to requests being sent to your ISP’s DNS servers.

Key takeaway: DNS leaks happen when DNS requests escape the VPN tunnel, exposing your browsing activity to third parties.

Limitations and edge cases

While most VPNs aim to prevent DNS leaks, several limitations and edge cases exist. Some VPNs don’t support IPv6, causing leaks if your ISP and device use it. Others may not fully integrate with your operating system’s DNS settings, especially on platforms like Android or routers. Additionally, certain network configurations, such as split tunnelling or custom DNS setups, can inadvertently cause leaks.

Key takeaway: No VPN is flawless; understanding your device and network environment is key to managing DNS leaks effectively.

How to test DNS/IPv6/WebRTC leaks

Testing for leaks is the first step in diagnosing whether your VPN is protecting your DNS queries properly. Several online tools allow you to check for DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC leaks by analysing your visible IP addresses and DNS servers. Below is a comparison table of popular leak testing websites:

Fixes by platform

Windows

  • Disable IPv6 via Network Adapter settings to prevent IPv6 leaks.
  • Manually set DNS servers to trusted ones (e.g., Google DNS 8.8.8.8, Cloudflare 1.1.1.1) in Network Properties.
  • Enable any DNS leak protection features in your VPN client.
  • Use the Windows firewall or third-party tools to block DNS requests outside the VPN tunnel.

macOS

  • Disable IPv6 in Network Preferences or configure it carefully.
  • Manually configure DNS servers to secure options in System Preferences > Network.
  • Use VPN clients with built-in leak protection; some third-party apps offer advanced network controls.
  • Flush DNS cache regularly using Terminal commands (e.g., sudo killall -HUP mDNSResponder).

Android

  • Use VPN apps that support DNS leak protection and IPv6 handling; avoid unreliable free VPNs (see free VPN options).
  • Manually configure private DNS (DNS over TLS) under network settings.
  • Disable IPv6 if possible, or use apps that block IPv6 traffic outside the VPN.
  • Consider rooting the device for advanced DNS control, but this carries risks.

iOS

  • Use reputable VPN apps with integrated DNS leak protection (see VPN for iOS guides).
  • Configure DNS manually via Wi-Fi settings for trusted DNS servers.
  • Disable WebRTC in Safari or use browsers that block WebRTC leaks.
  • Keep the device updated to benefit from the latest network security fixes.

Router

  • Change the router DNS servers to secure, no-logging providers.
  • Ensure the router firmware supports DNS leak protection or install custom firmware like DD-WRT or OpenWrt.
  • Disable IPv6 if your VPN does not support it, or configure IPv6 tunnels carefully.
  • Set up VPN at the router level to cover all connected devices, reducing chances of leaks.

Troubleshooting checklist

  1. Verify VPN connection is active and stable.
  2. Run DNS leak tests on multiple sites like ipleak.net and dnsleaktest.com.
  3. Check for IPv6 leaks and disable IPv6 if unsupported by your VPN.
  4. Manually set DNS servers on your device or router to trusted providers.
  5. Enable DNS leak protection features in your VPN client.
  6. Flush DNS cache on your device to clear old queries.
  7. Test WebRTC leaks in your browser and disable WebRTC if leaking.
  8. Update your VPN client and device OS to the latest versions.
  9. Consider switching to a VPN with better leak protection if problems persist.
  10. Avoid free VPNs with poor DNS handling; see the discussion on free vs paid VPNs.
  11. Check router settings or upgrade firmware to support leak protection.
  12. Use firewall rules to block DNS traffic outside the VPN tunnel.

FAQ

What exactly is a DNS leak?

A DNS leak occurs when your DNS queries, which translate domain names into IP addresses, are sent outside the encrypted VPN tunnel and reach your ISP or other third parties, potentially exposing your browsing activity despite the VPN connection.

Can DNS leaks reveal my real IP address?

While DNS leaks primarily expose the DNS server used, they can indirectly reveal your true IP or location by showing your ISP’s DNS servers. Some leaks may also reveal your IPv6 address if not properly handled.

Are all VPNs vulnerable to DNS leaks?

Most VPNs aim to prevent DNS leaks, but vulnerabilities exist especially in free or poorly configured VPNs. It depends on the VPN’s client, protocols used, and how well it handles DNS and IPv6 traffic.

How do I know if my VPN is leaking DNS?

You can test for leaks using websites like dnsleaktest.com or ipleak.net. These tools will show which DNS servers your device is using when connected to the VPN.

Is disabling IPv6 a good permanent solution?

Disabling IPv6 can prevent leaks if your VPN doesn’t support it, but it’s more of a workaround than a fix. Ideally, use a VPN that properly handles IPv6 traffic to avoid losing functionality or security.

Can browser settings cause DNS or WebRTC leaks?

Yes, browsers can leak your IP through WebRTC or DNS prefetching. Using privacy-focused browsers or extensions that block WebRTC and disabling DNS prefetching can help reduce leaks.

Should I be worried about DNS leaks if I only use a VPN for streaming?

While DNS leaks might not be critical for casual streaming, they can still expose your location or ISP. If you want to access geo-blocked content reliably and privately, it’s worth ensuring your VPN doesn’t leak DNS.
