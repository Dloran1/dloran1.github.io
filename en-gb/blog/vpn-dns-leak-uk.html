Using a VPN is an excellent way to enhance your online privacy and security. However, even when connected to a VPN, your DNS requests—the queries that translate website names into IP addresses—can sometimes bypass the encrypted tunnel and leak to your ISP or other third parties. This phenomenon, known as a DNS leak, can undermine the privacy benefits of a VPN.

Understanding how DNS leaks occur and how to detect and fix them is crucial for anyone serious about maintaining online anonymity. This guide covers the essentials for 2026, including testing methods, platform-specific fixes, and troubleshooting advice to keep your DNS queries secure.

Quick answer

  • DNS leaks happen when DNS requests bypass the VPN tunnel and are sent to your ISP or other unauthorised DNS servers.
  • Common causes include misconfigured VPN clients, IPv6 leaks, or the use of default DNS settings on your device.
  • Testing tools like dnsleaktest.com or ipleak.net help identify leaks.
  • Fixes vary by platform but typically involve changing DNS settings, disabling IPv6, or using VPN clients with built-in leak protection.

When does a DNS leak happen?

Key takeaway: A DNS leak occurs whenever your DNS queries are routed outside the VPN’s encrypted tunnel, exposing your browsing activity to your ISP or other observers. This can happen due to VPN client bugs, operating system behaviours, or network configuration issues.

DNS leaks often occur when a VPN fails to properly reroute DNS requests through its own servers. For example, some VPNs only tunnel IPv4 traffic but not IPv6, which can cause DNS queries over IPv6 to leak. Additionally, if your device is configured to use system or router DNS servers rather than those provided by the VPN, DNS leaks may arise.

It’s also worth noting that some free VPNs or poorly designed VPN services may not prioritise DNS leak protection, so users should be cautious. For more information on VPN quality differences, you might want to read about free vs paid VPNs.

Limitations and edge cases

Key takeaway: Although DNS leak protection is essential, no VPN can guarantee complete anonymity or prevent all forms of data leakage. DNS leaks are just one of several potential privacy issues that users should be aware of.

Some edge cases include complex network setups where multiple DNS servers or proxies are in use, or when VPN clients clash with firewall or antivirus software. In certain corporate or public networks, captive portals or forced DNS redirects can also cause leaks. Moreover, some VPNs do not fully support IPv6, leading to leaks despite IPv4 protection.

It’s important to complement VPN use with other privacy best practices, such as using no-logs VPNs and understanding the limitations discussed in no logs VPN articles.

How to test DNS/IPv6/WebRTC leaks

Leak Type Test Website or Tool What It Detects
DNS Leak dnsleaktest.com DNS servers your system is using; checks if DNS queries are routed outside the VPN tunnel
IPv6 Leak ipleak.net Whether IPv6 traffic is leaking despite VPN connection (common if VPN does not support IPv6)
WebRTC Leak ipleak.net Detects if browser WebRTC requests expose your real IP address outside the VPN

Key takeaway: Regularly testing for DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC leaks using trusted online tools is a straightforward way to check your VPN’s privacy effectiveness. Testing before and after applying fixes will confirm if leaks have been resolved.

Fixes by platform

Windows

  • Disable IPv6 in network adapter settings to prevent IPv6 leaks if your VPN doesn’t support IPv6 fully.
  • Manually set DNS servers to those provided by your VPN or trusted third parties like Cloudflare (1.1.1.1) or Google DNS (8.8.8.8).
  • Ensure your VPN client has DNS leak protection enabled; check settings or preferences.
  • Enable the VPN kill switch to block traffic if the VPN connection drops, preventing accidental leaks.
  • Consider using firewall rules to restrict DNS requests to VPN DNS servers only.

macOS

  • Disable IPv6 via Terminal commands or network preferences if unsupported by your VPN.
  • Set custom DNS servers in Network Preferences aligned with your VPN provider.
  • Use VPN clients with built-in DNS leak protection; some third-party apps offer better leak prevention than native macOS VPN settings.
  • Flush DNS cache regularly using sudo killall -HUP mDNSResponder to clear old DNS data.

Android

  • Use VPN apps from reputable providers that explicitly state DNS leak protection.
  • Disable IPv6 support if your VPN app or network setup doesn’t handle it properly.
  • Manually configure private DNS settings (e.g., DNS over TLS) where supported to ensure DNS encryption.
  • Keep your device’s OS updated to benefit from the latest security fixes.
  • Review permissions and background apps to avoid DNS leaks from other apps bypassing the VPN.

iOS

  • Use VPN apps with DNS leak protection features; Apple's built-in VPN settings are limited.
  • Disable IPv6 if your VPN provider advises it to prevent leaks.
  • Configure private DNS settings if your VPN supports it.
  • Regularly test for leaks and restart your device after changing VPN or DNS settings.

Router

  • Set your router’s DNS servers to those provided by your VPN or trusted DNS providers.
  • Disable IPv6 on your router if your VPN does not support it or leaks IPv6 traffic.
  • Flash your router with custom firmware (e.g., DD-WRT, OpenWrt) for better VPN and DNS control if needed.
  • Ensure your router’s VPN client configuration includes DNS leak protection settings.

Troubleshooting checklist

  1. Connect to your VPN and visit dnsleaktest.com to check for DNS leaks.
  2. Test for IPv6 leaks on ipleak.net and disable IPv6 if leaks are detected.
  3. Verify WebRTC leaks using browser tests on ipleak.net and disable WebRTC or use browser extensions if necessary.
  4. Ensure your VPN client’s DNS leak protection feature is enabled in settings.
  5. Manually configure DNS servers in your device or router settings to trusted providers.
  6. Flush DNS cache on your device to clear old or cached DNS information.
  7. Check for conflicting software such as firewalls or antivirus that may interfere with VPN DNS routing.
  8. Restart your device and VPN client after making changes.
  9. Update your VPN client and device OS to the latest versions.
  10. Try connecting to different VPN servers to rule out server-specific issues.
  11. Consider switching to a VPN known for strong DNS leak protection; see recommendations on the best free VPN UK page.
  12. If using a router VPN, double-check VPN and DNS settings or consult the router’s manual.

FAQ

What exactly is a DNS leak?

A DNS leak happens when DNS queries, which translate website names into IP addresses, bypass the VPN tunnel and are sent to your ISP or other DNS servers. This reveals the websites you visit even though your VPN is connected.

Can a DNS leak expose my real IP address?

Not directly, but it can reveal your browsing activity and location to your ISP or network observer. If combined with other leaks like IPv6 or WebRTC leaks, it might contribute to exposing your real IP.

How do I know if my VPN has DNS leak protection?

Check your VPN client settings for DNS leak protection features or consult the provider’s documentation. You can also run leak tests on sites like dnsleaktest.com to verify.

Does disabling IPv6 help prevent DNS leaks?

Yes, many VPN services do not fully support IPv6, so disabling it on your device can prevent leaks over that protocol. However, some VPNs now support IPv6 natively, so check your provider’s capabilities.

Are free VPNs more likely to have DNS leaks?

Often, yes. Free VPNs may lack robust DNS leak protection or use shared DNS servers that increase leak risk. For a balanced view, see the comparison between free vs paid VPNs.

Can I fix DNS leaks on all devices?

Generally yes, but the methods differ by platform. Desktop operating systems offer more control, while mobile devices and routers may require specific apps or firmware updates. Detailed fixes are outlined above.

What is a VPN kill switch and does it stop DNS leaks?

A kill switch blocks internet traffic if the VPN connection drops, preventing your real IP or DNS requests from leaking. While it doesn’t fix leaks during active connections, it is a vital safety net.

Should I use third-party DNS providers with my VPN?

Using trusted DNS providers like Cloudflare or Google DNS can help prevent leaks if your VPN’s DNS servers are unreliable. However, the best option is to use your VPN’s DNS or encrypted DNS services when available.
