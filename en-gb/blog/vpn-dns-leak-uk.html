Using a VPN is a reliable way to enhance your online privacy by encrypting your internet traffic and masking your IP address. However, even the best VPNs can suffer from DNS leaks, which undermine your anonymity by exposing your DNS queries to your internet service provider (ISP) or other third parties. Understanding how DNS leaks happen and how to detect and fix them is essential to maintaining your privacy in 2026.

DNS leaks are often overlooked but can reveal more about your browsing habits than you might realise. Whether you’re streaming geo-blocked content or simply browsing securely, it’s worth ensuring your VPN is properly configured to prevent these leaks. This guide covers the essentials of DNS leaks, how to test for them, and practical fixes across different platforms.

Quick answer

  • A DNS leak occurs when your DNS queries bypass the VPN tunnel and are sent to your ISP or other DNS servers, exposing your browsing activity.
  • Leaks often happen due to improper VPN configuration, IPv6 traffic, or WebRTC vulnerabilities in browsers.
  • Testing for leaks involves specialised websites that check DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC information.
  • Fixes vary by platform but generally include disabling IPv6, changing DNS settings, and using VPN kill switches.

When does a DNS leak happen?

DNS leaks typically occur when your device sends DNS requests outside the encrypted VPN tunnel. This can happen if your VPN client does not properly route DNS queries through its own secure servers or if your operating system defaults to using ISP DNS servers. Additionally, IPv6 traffic and browser features like WebRTC can cause leaks, even if your VPN is connected. The key takeaway is that a VPN connection alone does not guarantee DNS privacy unless the VPN and device are configured correctly.

Limitations and edge cases

While most VPN providers strive to prevent DNS leaks, some limitations remain. For example, IPv6 support is inconsistent across VPN services, and many still only handle IPv4 traffic properly. Edge cases include split tunnelling configurations and certain network environments where VPN clients struggle to enforce DNS routing. Also, some free or less reputable VPNs may not prioritise leak protection, increasing risk. It’s important to remain realistic: no VPN can promise perfect anonymity, but understanding these limitations helps manage expectations and improve privacy.

How to test DNS/IPv6/WebRTC leaks

Testing for leaks is a crucial step in verifying your VPN’s effectiveness. Several online tools allow you to check if your DNS queries, IPv6 traffic, or WebRTC IP addresses are exposed.

Key takeaway: Regularly testing for DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC leaks helps you identify vulnerabilities in your VPN setup and ensures your privacy remains intact.

Fixes by platform

Windows

  • Disable IPv6: Go to Network & Internet settings > Change adapter options > Right-click your VPN adapter > Properties > Uncheck Internet Protocol Version 6 (TCP/IPv6).
  • Manually set DNS servers: Use your VPN’s recommended DNS or a privacy-focused DNS like Cloudflare (1.1.1.1) or Google DNS (8.8.8.8).
  • Enable the VPN kill switch if available to block internet traffic if the VPN drops.
  • Disable WebRTC in browsers by using extensions or browser settings (e.g., Firefox’s privacy.resistFingerprinting setting).

macOS

  • Turn off IPv6 in System Preferences > Network > Advanced > TCP/IP > Configure IPv6 > Off or Link-local only.
  • Configure DNS servers manually to use VPN DNS or trusted third-party DNS.
  • Use VPN apps that integrate kill switches and leak protection.
  • Disable WebRTC in Safari or use privacy-focused browsers such as Firefox with appropriate extensions.

Android

  • Use VPN apps with built-in DNS leak protection and kill switches.
  • Disable IPv6 if your VPN doesn’t support it or your device allows it (varies by Android version).
  • Manually configure private DNS in Settings > Network > Private DNS (use a secure provider).
  • Use browsers that allow disabling WebRTC or install apps designed to block WebRTC leaks.

iOS

  • Choose VPN apps with DNS leak protection and kill switches.
  • Disable IPv6 by configuring VPN profiles that do not route IPv6 traffic.
  • Set DNS via VPN app settings or use system-level DNS overrides where possible.
  • Use Safari’s privacy features and consider browsers with WebRTC leak protection.

Router

  • Flash compatible router firmware (e.g., OpenWRT, DD-WRT) that supports VPN client functionality.
  • Disable IPv6 or configure it to route through the VPN tunnel.
  • Set DNS servers on the router to your VPN provider’s DNS or trusted third-party DNS.
  • Enable firewall rules to block DNS requests outside the VPN tunnel.

Troubleshooting checklist

  1. Verify your VPN is connected and the server location matches your intended region.
  2. Run DNS leak tests on multiple websites to confirm no DNS requests leak.
  3. Check for IPv6 leaks and disable IPv6 if your VPN does not support it.
  4. Test WebRTC leaks in your browser and apply browser-specific fixes if necessary.
  5. Ensure your VPN client has the latest updates and leak protection features enabled.
  6. Manually configure DNS servers if your VPN’s DNS leaks or is slow.
  7. Enable and test your VPN kill switch to prevent traffic leaks if the VPN drops.
  8. Restart your device and router to clear any cached DNS settings.
  9. Consider switching VPN protocols if leaks persist (e.g., OpenVPN to WireGuard).
  10. Review your firewall or security software settings to ensure they don’t interfere with VPN routing.
  11. Consult your VPN provider’s support or knowledge base for platform-specific instructions.
  12. Test your VPN on other devices to isolate if the leak is device-specific.

FAQ

What exactly is a DNS leak?

A DNS leak happens when your DNS queries—which translate website names into IP addresses—are sent outside your VPN’s encrypted tunnel. This means your ISP or other observers can see which websites you visit, even though your IP address is masked.

Can free VPNs cause more DNS leaks?

Yes, many free VPNs have weaker leak protection or unreliable DNS handling, increasing the risk of leaks. For better privacy, consider reputable paid VPNs, as discussed in our free vs paid VPN UK comparison.

How does IPv6 contribute to DNS leaks?

IPv6 traffic can bypass VPN tunnels if the VPN does not support it properly, revealing your real IP address and DNS queries. Disabling IPv6 on your device or VPN client is a common fix.

What is WebRTC and why is it a privacy concern?

WebRTC is a browser feature that enables real-time communication but can expose your real IP address via STUN requests, even when connected to a VPN. Disabling or blocking WebRTC can prevent this leak.

Are VPN kill switches effective against DNS leaks?

VPN kill switches block all internet traffic if the VPN connection drops, preventing DNS leaks during downtime. They are a valuable feature for maintaining privacy but must be enabled manually in most VPN apps.

Can I test for leaks on my mobile device?

Absolutely. Many leak test websites are mobile-friendly, and you can also find dedicated apps that help check for DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC leaks on Android and iOS devices. For Android-specific guidance, see our VPN Android UK guide.

Will changing DNS servers improve leak protection?

Manually setting DNS servers to those provided by your VPN or privacy-focused providers can help reduce leaks. However, this only works if your VPN properly routes DNS queries through its tunnel.

Is it possible to have zero leaks with a VPN?

While it’s difficult to guarantee zero leaks due to technical and network complexities, following best practices and using a reputable VPN with strong leak protection can minimise risks significantly. See our no logs VPN UK article for more on privacy considerations.
