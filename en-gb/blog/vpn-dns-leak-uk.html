What Is a DNS Leak?

A DNS leak occurs when your device sends domain name queries outside the encrypted VPN tunnel, typically to your Internet Service Provider’s (ISP) DNS servers. This means that even if your VPN connection is active and your visible IP appears concealed, third parties or your ISP could still see which websites you visit by viewing your DNS queries. This unintended information disclosure undermines both your privacy and the core security advantage provided by using a VPN.

Key takeaway: A DNS leak exposes your browsing history to your ISP or outsiders, even if your VPN claims you’re protected.

How DNS Leaks Happen (2026 Update)

DNS leaks can occur for a variety of technical and human reasons. Here are some of the most common scenarios as of 2026, reflecting changes in operating systems, browsers, and the increasing complexity of home networks:

  • VPN Misconfiguration: If your VPN’s software does not properly route DNS queries through its own encrypted servers, your device may fall back to default ISP DNS servers.
  • OS-Level Settings: Windows, macOS, and even some mobile OSes aggressively cache and resolve DNS, sometimes overriding VPN settings after updates.
  • IPv6 Traffic: If your VPN only blocks or tunnels IPv4 DNS (the older address format), some DNS traffic over IPv6 may leak past the VPN.
  • WebRTC in Browsers: Modern browsers (especially Chromium-based ones) can reveal your real IP and DNS via WebRTC, even if your VPN is otherwise correctly configured.
  • Routers: Home routers may push their own DNS settings even after a VPN connection is established on a device.
Key takeaway: DNS leaks are often caused by a mix of software updates, overlooked IPv6, browser features, or restrictive device/ISP setups.

How to Detect a DNS Leak

Detecting a DNS leak is essential to verify your privacy claims and can be done in under three minutes. Use reputable DNS leak test websites or open-source tools for an accurate diagnosis.

  1. Activate your VPN: Connect to your VPN and ensure it displays an active connection to the desired country.
  2. Visit a DNS Leak Test site: Use a site such as dnsleaktest.com or similar. These sites display the IP addresses and hostnames of DNS servers your browser is using.
  3. Perform a standard and “extended” test: The extended test checks more query types. Watch for any DNS server listed under your actual ISP or local geography (not the VPN provider’s network or chosen country).
  4. Repeat on all browsers: Don’t assume all browsers behave the same—check each one separately, especially if extensions or proxies are in use.

On mobile devices, some advanced DNS leaks may only be detectable using testing apps from the app store, due to mobile browser sandboxes.

Types of Leaks: DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC

Beyond regular DNS leaks, two further issues now require attention in 2026: IPv6 and WebRTC leaks. These add extra vectors for privacy loss.

DNS Leak DNS queries go to non-VPN DNS servers Visit DNS leak test sites, check for ISP info

Preventing “All at Once” Leaks: Some VPN clients offer settings to block DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC leaks in-app. If not, you may need adjustments per device or browser.

Key takeaway: DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC leaks all risk revealing your real location despite VPN protection.

How to Fix DNS Leaks (Windows, macOS, Android, iOS, Routers)

Below are step-by-step fixes for DNS leaks as of 2026. These address not just classic DNS but also IPv6 and WebRTC exposures. Pick your platform:

Windows

  • Use a VPN with “DNS leak protection”: Check VPN settings for an explicit toggle or feature.
  • Manually set DNS servers: Go to Network & Internet Settings > Change Adapter Options. Right-click your adapter > Properties > IPv4 Properties. Enter DNS servers (e.g. 1.1.1.1 for Cloudflare, 9.9.9.9 for Quad9) instead of “automatic”.
  • Disable IPv6: In Adapter > Properties, untick “Internet Protocol Version 6 (TCP/IPv6)”.
  • WebRTC: For Edge/Chrome, add extensions like “WebRTC Leak Prevent”. In Firefox, go to about:config and set media.peerconnection.enabled to false.

macOS

  • Use a reliable VPN client: Prefer OpenVPN, WireGuard, or the app recommended by your VPN provider.
  • Custom DNS: System Preferences > Network > Advanced > DNS. Remove all entries and add secure DNS (Cloudflare, Quad9, or your VPN's).
  • Turn off IPv6: In Terminal: networksetup -setv6off Wi-Fi (replace Wi-Fi as needed).
  • WebRTC: Use Safari or configure WebRTC-blocking extensions/browsers.

Android & iOS

  • Trusted VPN app required: Only use verified apps, enabling any “leak protection” or “private DNS” features within them.
  • Android Private DNS: Set to a secure DNS provider under Network & Internet > Advanced > Private DNS.
  • iOS WebRTC: Safari restricts most WebRTC leaks, but for privacy add-ons, see the App Store.
  • Disable IPv6 (Android, advanced): Some devices allow this under system network settings; if not, rely on VPN’s split-tunnel control.

Routers (Home/Office)

  • VPN at router level: Install OpenVPN or WireGuard on the router and set the router's DNS to a privacy-respecting third-party.
  • Disable IPv6 in router admin panel: Especially if your VPN does not claim IPv6 support.
  • Custom firmware: Consider flashing routers with DD-WRT/OpenWRT for maximum DNS and VPN control.
Key takeaway: Fixes depend on your devices, but turning off IPv6 and using VPN DNS are universally safe steps.

Limits of Protection: Realistic Privacy in 2026

While mitigating DNS leaks is crucial, even the best VPN or operational security cannot make users invisible. In 2026, authorities and major tech firms remain able to correlate traffic using time, device behaviour, or browser fingerprinting. ISPs might inspect traffic patterns, and many websites use aggressive tracking that’s not DNS-based.

  • Public Wi-Fi networks may log device identifiers or MAC addresses.
  • Browser fingerprinting works independently of network-level privacy.
  • VPN providers themselves could be compromised or forced to provide information.

Continued vigilance—regularly updating software, carefully choosing your VPN, and understanding the difference between “anonymity” and “privacy”—remains necessary.

Key takeaway: DNS leak prevention is part of the puzzle; true privacy means layering defences and understanding limitations.

FAQ: DNS Leak with VPN

Can I get a DNS leak on any VPN?
Yes, no VPN is 100% immune. Misconfiguration, client bugs, or software updates may cause leaks even with reputable VPN providers.
If my VPN advertises “no logs”, should I still check for DNS leaks?
Yes. “No logs” refers to not keeping usage records, not necessarily to traffic routing. Trust but verify, especially after VPN or OS updates.
Is using Google or Cloudflare DNS safe with VPN?
They are more private than ISP DNS, but your queries are still visible to those companies unless fully routed via VPN. For maximum privacy, use your VPN’s dedicated DNS servers.
Do browser extensions alone protect against DNS & WebRTC leaks?
No, extensions can only patch browser-level leaks (like WebRTC) and have no control over OS- or device-level DNS leaks.
Can DNS leaks reveal passwords or the content I transmit?
No, only the domain names you access—not the full URLs or contents—are leaked, but this still reveals your interests or sensitive site visits.
Should I use a multi-hop VPN or “double VPN” to stop DNS leaks?
While multi-hop adds a layer of privacy, it does not always address DNS leaks unless both endpoints enforce DNS and IPv6 controls.

Conclusion

An up-to-date VPN is a cornerstone of online privacy, but by itself is not a “set and forget” solution. DNS leaks continue to threaten user anonymity in 2026, assisted by OS quirks, browser features like WebRTC, or advancing IPv6 adoption. Regular testing, proper VPN configuration, and understanding device-specific risks are vital. Fix leaks early, understand the limits, and keep reviewing your privacy setup as technology evolves.
