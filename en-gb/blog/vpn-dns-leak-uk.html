Using a VPN is one of the most popular ways to protect your online privacy. But even if your VPN is enabled and running, your private information could still ‘leak’ due to DNS requests escaping the encrypted tunnel. This phenomenon, called a DNS leak, exposes your browsing activity to your internet service provider or anyone monitoring your network.

This guide explains what DNS leaks are, how and when they happen, how to detect and fix them, and the real limitations of VPN privacy in 2026. Whether you’re using Windows, macOS, Android, iOS, or configuring a router, you’ll find actionable advice for plugging DNS leaks and bolstering your privacy.

Quick answer

  • A DNS leak occurs when DNS requests bypass your VPN and are visible to your internet provider or other third parties.
  • You can test for DNS leaks using online tools or by inspecting your network traffic.
  • Common DNS leak fixes include enabling VPN ‘kill switches’, modifying network settings, and using DNS leak protection features.
  • Leaks can also occur through IPv6 and WebRTC, requiring additional precautions.
  • Each platform (Windows, macOS, Android, iOS) has unique vulnerabilities and solutions.
Key takeaway: DNS leaks are a significant privacy risk, but you can detect and fix them on any device with the right tools and techniques.

When does a DNS leak happen?

A DNS leak typically happens when DNS queries bypass the VPN tunnel and are routed through the default DNS servers assigned by your internet service provider (ISP). This can occur in several real-world scenarios:

  • VPN misconfiguration: The VPN client fails to override system DNS settings, causing some or all requests to use your default DNS.
  • OS-level behaviour: Some operating systems, especially Windows, may send parallel DNS requests outside the VPN due to ‘Smart Multi-Homed Name Resolution’ or similar features.
  • Manual network changes: Manually setting custom DNS settings, or disabling a VPN without reapplying correct settings, can cause leaks.
  • Interruption or VPN crash: If the VPN disconnects unexpectedly, normal DNS routing may resume.
  • Dual-stack networks (IPv4 + IPv6): If your VPN only secures IPv4, IPv6 traffic (including DNS) may leak.

Increasingly, third-party apps and browsers with their own networking capabilities (like certain custom browsers or background services) can also initiate DNS queries that skip the VPN's paths.

Key takeaway: DNS leaks are most often caused by operating system or VPN misconfigurations, VPN disconnects, or network features that bypass encrypted tunnels.

Limitations and edge cases of VPN privacy

Even with a properly configured VPN, DNS leaks are only part of the broader privacy puzzle. There are inherent platform limitations and edge cases which every privacy-seeker should be aware of:

  • Operating system influence: Some operating systems, like Windows, prioritise speed over privacy by resolving DNS queries via multiple interfaces.
  • Browser and app behaviour: Browsers with ‘secure DNS’ (DNS over HTTPS) or ‘split tunnelling’ features may use different DNS servers.
  • WebRTC leaks: Even if your DNS requests are secure, WebRTC can reveal your real IP address via STUN requests, particularly in browsers that haven't disabled WebRTC or lack effective privacy controls.
  • IPv6 traffic: Many VPNs only support IPv4 by default. DNS queries and IP requests using IPv6 might never go through the VPN at all unless explicitly supported or blocked.
Common VPN leak types beyond DNS

Some VPNs now include leak protections for these threats, but complete anonymity is rarely guaranteed. ISPs, websites, and even local networks may still be able to infer your identity or activity in some situations.

Key takeaway: VPNs can protect most DNS queries, but platform quirks, IPv6, WebRTC, and app-level leaks pose ongoing privacy challenges even with a solid VPN setup.

How to test for DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC leaks

Testing for leaks is the most reliable way to verify your VPN configuration. A test-driven approach ensures your DNS and IP addresses aren’t being exposed unintentionally. Here’s how to conduct a comprehensive leak test in 2026:

  1. Connect your VPN and verify it shows you connected to your chosen server location.
  2. Find your VPN-assigned IP and DNS servers. Open your VPN software and note the IP/DNS addresses in use—these should not match your local ISP details.
  3. Use an online leak testing tool. Browse to a DNS/IP leak checker site that tests for DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC. These typically display a list of server addresses seen from your connection.
  4. Interpret the results:
    • If any IP or DNS address listed matches your real-world ISP or location, you have a leak.
    • For WebRTC, if your ‘real’ IP (public or local) appears, your browser is leaking this data.
    • If your IPv6 address is visible, and you don’t expect it, your VPN is not protecting IPv6.
  5. Check on different browsers and platforms, as browser-level behaviour or app DNS overrides may exist.

Repeat tests after network changes, OS upgrades, or VPN app updates, as new leaks can develop at any time.

Key takeaway: Regularly running DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC leak tests across browsers and devices is essential for maintaining VPN privacy.

DNS leak fixes by platform

Effective DNS leak prevention looks different across major devices and platforms. Here’s a practical guide to fixes for each:

Windows

  • Enable ‘DNS leak protection’ in your VPN client settings wherever possible.
  • Disable ‘Smart Multi-Homed Name Resolution’ via Group Policy or registry edits (causes parallel DNS lookup).
  • Set static DNS servers matching your VPN provider's recommendations.
  • Turn off IPv6 if your VPN does not support it—use the ‘Network and Sharing Center’ panel.

macOS

  • Use VPN software that fully integrates with macOS and overwrites DNS settings.
  • Test the VPN both in the app and when configured in the system ‘Network’ panel.
  • Disable IPv6 via the Terminal if unsupported (‘networksetup -setv6off’ command).

Android

  • Choose VPNs supporting ‘Always-on VPN’ or ‘block connections without VPN’ in network settings.
  • Avoid using ‘Private DNS’ with custom entries unless it’s aligned with your VPN provider.
  • Update apps for IPv6 awareness and use browsers with WebRTC control features.

iOS

  • Opt for reputable VPN apps that use the native ‘Tunnel’ or ‘IKEv2’ protocols rather than manual configurations.
  • Test with various Wi-Fi networks to see if DNS requests leak on certain captive portals.
  • Note: Disabling IPv6 is rarely possible without jailbreaking.

Router

  • Use VPN routers running compatible firmware (like OpenWrt, DD-WRT, or vendor-specific firmware).
  • Set custom DNS entries at the router level, ensuring all LAN clients use the VPN's DNS.
  • Block all outgoing UDP/53 and TCP/53 (DNS) traffic except to your specified DNS servers where possible.

Most modern VPNs add ‘kill switch’ features—ensure this is enabled to prevent any connections if the VPN drops.

Key takeaway: DNS leak solutions vary by platform, but centre on correct VPN settings, disabling IPv6 if needed, and ensuring all DNS is resolved within the secure tunnel.

DNS leak troubleshooting checklist

If you suspect a DNS (or other) leak, use this structured troubleshooting list to identify and resolve the problem:

  1. Run a DNS leak test and record the DNS servers listed.
  2. Compare these to your ISP’s and your VPN’s DNS settings.
  3. Reconnect your VPN and repeat the test. Clear your browser cache to eliminate old connections.
  4. Check VPN app settings for a ‘DNS leak protection’ toggle or similar feature; enable it.
  5. If IPv6 addresses appear, disable IPv6 at the operating system or router level unless your VPN supports it.
  6. For browser-based WebRTC leaks, disable WebRTC or limit peer connections in browser settings (especially on Chromium-based browsers).
  7. Manually configure static DNS within the VPN client or use the app’s custom DNS feature if available.
  8. Update your VPN app and firmware regularly to patch known leak vulnerabilities.
  9. If the VPN disconnects or reconnects frequently, use a ‘kill switch’ option or firewall rules to block non-VPN DNS.
  10. For persistent leaks, contact VPN provider support for guidance, or consider using a different service more compatible with your platform.
Key takeaway: Diagnosing DNS leaks is a step-by-step process—test, adjust settings, disable IPv6 or WebRTC if needed, and always keep VPN software updated.

FAQ: DNS Leaks and VPN Use in 2026

What is a DNS leak?
A DNS leak occurs when your system sends DNS requests outside the VPN tunnel, exposing the websites you visit to your ISP or third parties.
Can I have a DNS leak even when connected to a VPN?
Yes, misconfigurations or limitations in VPN apps or operating systems can still route DNS traffic outside the tunnel.
How do I know if my VPN has a DNS leak?
Run an online DNS leak test with your VPN connected. If any DNS server listed belongs to your ISP, you have a leak.
Why does my browser show my real IP despite using a VPN?
This is likely a WebRTC leak. Disable WebRTC in your browser settings, or use privacy-focused browsers that block WebRTC requests.
What causes DNS leaks on Windows 11?
Features like ‘Smart Multi-Homed Name Resolution’ and multiple active network interfaces can send DNS traffic outside the VPN tunnel.
Does disabling IPv6 stop all DNS leaks?
No, but it prevents one common leak route. You must still ensure your DNS queries use the VPN-assigned servers over IPv4.
Is it possible to stop WebRTC leaks on mobile browsers?
Some mobile browsers offer WebRTC controls in settings, but success varies; try using a privacy-focused browser as needed.
Can routers have DNS leaks even with a VPN setup?
Yes, if DNS queries bypass the VPN tunnel, such as if devices use hardcoded DNS or router misconfiguration occurs.
What is a ‘kill switch’ on a VPN?
A kill switch blocks all internet access if the VPN disconnects, ensuring no data, including DNS, can leak during outages.
Do all VPNs provide DNS leak protection by default?
No—a surprising number require you to enable this feature in the app’s advanced settings.
Is it enough to set my device’s DNS to a public DNS server (like Google or Cloudflare)?
No. Public DNS may still expose your queries to third parties unless DNS is routed through the VPN tunnel.
Will always-on VPN mode stop DNS leaks?
It helps—especially on Android and iOS—but you still need to test and confirm the VPN handles DNS correctly on your device.
Key takeaway: Regular DNS and WebRTC leak tests, paired with platform-specific solutions, are essential for reliable VPN privacy in 2026.

Every platform has unique risks, quirks, and options for plugging leaks. Stay updated on your VPN’s settings, use kill switches, disable unnecessary features like IPv6 and WebRTC when not essential, and always verify your configuration with leak tests. It’s an ongoing process, but essential for privacy-conscious users in a shifting digital landscape.
