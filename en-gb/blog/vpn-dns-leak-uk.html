DNS leaks are a common privacy risk that can undermine even the best VPN setups. When a DNS leak occurs, your true browsing history might be exposed to your ISP or network administrator, even if you’re using a VPN. While most modern VPNs promise to prevent these leaks, real-world configurations, device quirks, and human error can cause them to persist undetected.

This guide explains what a DNS leak is, why it matters for your privacy, and gives you actionable steps to detect and fix leaks. We focus on practical, device-specific solutions—with realistic advice about the technical and privacy limits you may encounter along the way.

Quick answer: What to do about DNS leaks

  • Run regular DNS leak tests after connecting to your VPN and after any major OS or VPN app update.
  • Watch out for IPv6 and WebRTC leaks—these may bypass VPN protections by default.
  • Fix leaks by changing your DNS server, disabling IPv6, or using built-in VPN leak protection settings.
  • Different platforms (Windows, macOS, Android, iOS, routers) each need specific configuration to prevent leaks.
  • Be aware: No VPN setup can guarantee 100% leak-proof privacy in all scenarios.
Key takeaway: DNS leaks expose your activity and should be checked and fixed on every device you use a VPN. There’s no one-size-fits-all solution—active testing and fine-tuning are required.

When and why do DNS leaks happen?

A DNS leak occurs when your device sends DNS queries outside of your encrypted VPN tunnel. This means your internet service provider (ISP) or any upstream network can see the websites you visit, even if they can’t see the exact content.

Common causes of DNS leaks include:

  • Operating systems using their default DNS settings even when the VPN is active.
  • VPN apps that don’t properly route DNS requests.
  • Split tunneling, incompatible browser extensions, and manual misconfiguration.
  • IPv6 traffic or WebRTC requests leaking outside the VPN tunnel.

Understanding these triggers is crucial, since preventing DNS leaks is as much about proper setup as it is about the VPN you choose.

Limits and edge cases: What a VPN can—and can’t—do for DNS leaks

While VPNs are meant to protect your DNS traffic, they have inherent limitations. Not all VPNs enforce DNS protection equally, and some system or browser features (like IPv6 auto-discovery or WebRTC) can slip through the cracks—even on a respected VPN. Additionally, device manufacturers and ISPs are constantly tweaking their networks, introducing fresh compatibility issues every year.

It’s critical to understand where VPN protection ends:

  • Shared DNS on public Wi-Fi: If network-level DNS hijacking occurs, a weak VPN config may not prevent leaks.
  • IPv6 and WebRTC: Not all VPNs or firewall rules account for these, leaving users exposed.
  • Mobile OS limitations: iOS and Android may restrict VPN apps from fully managing DNS on all apps or under certain power-saving modes.
  • Manual changes: User modifications to device settings (such as custom DNS or VPN split tunnelling) can inadvertently re-introduce leaks.

Technical solutions improve every year, but it's impossible to declare any VPN setup absolutely leak-proof, especially across all devices and networks.

How to test for DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC leaks

Testing for leaks is the only way to know if your setup is working. DNS leaks, IPv6 leaks, and WebRTC leaks are all different and need separate attention.

Here’s how to do a broad privacy leak check:

  1. Connect to your VPN. Choose an exit server in a different country for a clear distinction.
  2. Search online for 'DNS leak test', 'IPv6 leak test', and 'WebRTC leak test'. Open a leak test site on your primary browser. (If you use multiple browsers, test them all.)
  3. Run a 'Standard' and 'Extended' DNS leak test. Note which DNS servers are listed—if you see your ISP or home country, you may have a leak.
  4. Check the IPv6 leak test. If it shows your real IPv6 address, or any IPv6 address, while on a VPN that is not meant to support IPv6, that's likely a leak.
  5. Run a WebRTC leak test. If you see your real public IP or local IP, you have a WebRTC vulnerability, especially in browsers like Chrome or Firefox.

You can repeat these tests after changing settings, upgrading your VPN app, or connecting to a different network.

Key takeaway: Regular, manual testing is the only sure way to detect DNS, IPv6, or WebRTC leaks—don’t rely on VPN marketing claims alone.

How to fix DNS leaks: Windows, macOS, Android, iOS, and routers

The process for fixing DNS leaks differs by platform and device. Here’s how to patch vulnerabilities on each major system:

Windows

  • Disable IPv6: Go to Network Connections → Adapter properties → Uncheck Internet Protocol Version 6 (TCP/IPv6).
  • Set static DNS: Manually enter a trusted DNS like 1.1.1.1 or 9.9.9.9 in your network adapter.
  • Use 'Set DNS' features: Many VPN clients have built-in DNS leak protection—make sure it’s enabled in settings.
  • Block non-VPN traffic: Activate the VPN app’s firewall or kill switch, where available.

macOS

  • Force DNS: In System Preferences → Network, specify your DNS servers to a VPN provider’s values or public DNS.
  • Flush DNS after connect: Use "sudo killall -HUP mDNSResponder" in Terminal after connecting the VPN to clear any cached old DNS settings.
  • Disable IPv6 (advanced): Use Terminal command: "networksetup -setv6off [interface]" for the adapter.

Android

  • Enable 'Always-on VPN': This blocks apps from bypassing the VPN tunnel by default.
  • Use secure DNS (Private DNS): In Network & internet settings, configure “Private DNS” with a secure provider (e.g., dns.quad9.net).
  • Test after each system update: Some Android updates reset DNS or VPN prioritisation.

iOS

  • Use official VPN apps: Third-party (non-App Store) apps may not manage DNS properly.
  • Disable Local Network Access (where possible): In iOS privacy settings for each app.
  • Check VPN status bar: If it disappears, even briefly, DNS could leak before reconnection.

Routers

  • Configure router to use VPN DNS: Set your router to use your VPN provider's DNS or a secure public DNS.
  • Disable IPv6 on router: Many consumer routers have IPv6 enabled by default—turn it off if your VPN doesn’t support it.
  • Check routing tables: Ensure all outbound DNS queries are directed via the VPN.
  • Firmware updates: Outdated firmware may break DNS routing or leak traffic.
Key takeaway: Each OS and router has its own leak risks and fixes—manual intervention and regular retesting are vital for lasting privacy.

DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC troubleshooting checklist

Even after initial fixes, leaks can recur due to updates, app changes, or network switches. If you’re still seeing leaks after following the above solutions, troubleshoot with this checklist:

  • Disconnect and reconnect your VPN, then retest for leaks.
  • Reboot your device to clear cache and DNS settings.
  • Check that only your VPN’s DNS servers are listed in your network settings.
  • Clear browser DNS cache and history.
  • Disable WebRTC in your browser settings or via extensions.
  • Update your VPN app, device OS, and router firmware regularly.
  • Test with another browser or device for comparison.
  • Try a different VPN server location—some servers enforce DNS better than others.
  • Contact your VPN provider if problems persist.
Key takeaway: Don’t assume a fix is permanent—repeat leak tests after every major update or network change to maintain privacy.

FAQ: DNS leaks, privacy, and VPN support

What is a DNS leak?
A DNS leak is when your device sends website lookup requests outside of your VPN tunnel—often to your ISP’s DNS—revealing which sites you visit.
How often should I do DNS leak tests?
Run tests after installing your VPN, on first setup, after updates, and anytime you change network environments (home, work, public Wi-Fi).
Which is more serious: DNS, IPv6, or WebRTC leaks?
All can expose your identity, but DNS leaks are the most universally problematic because they reveal browsing history to third parties by default.
Why do VPNs sometimes fail to protect against DNS leaks?
Poor app design, OS restrictions, or edge-case network settings can cause a VPN to fail at rerouting DNS queries through encrypted tunnels.
Can a browser extension help?
Sometimes. Extensions can block WebRTC or force secure DNS in the browser, but they only protect that browser—not the whole device.
Is IPv6 support important for VPNs?
Yes. If a VPN doesn’t handle IPv6, the device might send IPv6 traffic outside the VPN—and leak your identity.
Do free VPNs prevent DNS leaks?
Rarely. Free VPNs often lack robust DNS leak protection, and some monetise leaks themselves.
How do I know if my DNS is leaking on my phone?
Use your phone browser to visit a DNS leak test site while connected to VPN. If your ISP or real location is revealed, you have a leak.
Do routers fix or cause DNS leaks?
Both. A properly configured VPN router can force all traffic (including DNS) through the VPN. But a misconfigured router can let leaks happen even if devices are secure.
Should I use public DNS like 1.1.1.1 or 8.8.8.8 with a VPN?
Using public DNS is safer than using your ISP, but the ideal is to route all DNS through your VPN servers to avoid correlation attacks.
What if I use Smart DNS services?
Smart DNS intentionally leaks some DNS to let you unblock geo-content, sacrificing privacy for convenience. Avoid if privacy is your priority.
Can I be 100% leak-proof?
No system is perfect; ongoing vigilance and periodic testing are the only ways to minimise DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC leaks.
Key takeaway: DNS leaks can affect any user, and no setup is immune—regular tests and proactive fixes are the best defence.

Conclusion

DNS leaks are a key weakness in any privacy setup, regardless of your VPN provider or tech skill. System and network oddities, updates, and user error all contribute to the risk. True privacy isn’t a one-time fix: it requires regular DNS (and IPv6/WebRTC) leak tests on every device, after every update or change in network. Each OS and router brings unique challenges, so consult platform-specific practices and remain sceptical of “leak-proof” marketing promises.

Stay proactive. By following the detection steps, applying the right fixes for your platform, and repeating tests as your environment changes, you can dramatically minimise your leak risk and enjoy the strongest privacy possible under real-world conditions in 2026.

Key takeaway: Vigilant testing and device-specific setup are required to keep DNS leaks under control—even for expert VPN users. Make leak-checking a core privacy habit.
