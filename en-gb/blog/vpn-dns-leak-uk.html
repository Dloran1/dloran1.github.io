Understanding DNS leaks is crucial for anyone using a VPN, especially in the UK where privacy concerns are increasingly relevant. A DNS leak occurs when your device bypasses the VPN tunnel and sends DNS requests directly to your internet service provider (ISP) or another unintended DNS server. This undermines the privacy and security benefits that a VPN aims to provide.

While VPNs are designed to mask your IP address and encrypt your internet traffic, DNS leaks can inadvertently reveal your browsing activity or location. Knowing how to detect, prevent, and fix these leaks is essential to maintaining a more private and secure online experience.

Quick answer

  • A DNS leak happens when DNS queries bypass the VPN tunnel, exposing your browsing activity to your ISP or third parties.
  • Leaks can occur due to misconfigured VPN clients, operating system behaviours, or IPv6 and WebRTC vulnerabilities.
  • Testing for leaks regularly using trusted websites helps identify if your VPN is protecting your DNS requests properly.
  • Fixes vary by platform and may include disabling IPv6, configuring VPN DNS settings, or using firewall rules.

When does a DNS leak happen?

Key takeaway: A DNS leak occurs when your device sends DNS requests outside the encrypted VPN tunnel, often due to default network settings or VPN client limitations. This typically happens on operating systems that prioritise local DNS servers or when IPv6 is enabled but not handled by the VPN.

DNS leaks frequently arise when a VPN client only routes IP traffic but neglects DNS queries, or when the operating system or browser uses protocols like WebRTC to reveal local IP details. Certain VPNs, especially free or poorly configured ones, may not fully protect against these leaks. For more on VPN risks, see the disadvantages of VPNs in the UK.

Limitations and edge cases

Key takeaway: Not all DNS leaks are straightforward to detect or fix. Some leaks happen intermittently or only under specific network conditions, such as switching between Wi-Fi and mobile data, or when using split tunnelling features.

VPNs cannot guarantee perfect anonymity or security; DNS leak protection is just one aspect of a comprehensive privacy strategy. Additionally, IPv6 traffic can bypass VPN tunnels if the provider does not support or disable it, and browser features like WebRTC can expose IP addresses despite the VPN. For a broader understanding of VPN myths and realities, see VPN myths in the UK.

How to test DNS/IPv6/WebRTC leaks

Regular testing is vital to ensure your VPN is not leaking DNS requests or IP addresses. Use reputable websites that specialise in leak detection. Below is a comparison of popular leak test sites and their features:

Website Leak Types Tested Notes
dnsleaktest.com DNS leaks (IPv4 & IPv6) Simple interface, quick standard and extended tests

BrowserLeaks.com WebRTC, DNS, IP leaks Comprehensive browser fingerprinting and leak tests

Key takeaway: Use multiple leak test sites to cover different leak types. Testing before and after connecting your VPN helps identify if leaks occur due to VPN configuration or your device’s network settings.

Fixes by platform

Windows

  • Disable IPv6 in Network Adapter settings if your VPN does not support it.
  • Manually configure VPN DNS servers in the VPN client or network adapter properties.
  • Use the Windows Firewall to block DNS requests outside the VPN tunnel.
  • Enable the VPN’s kill switch feature to prevent traffic leaks when the VPN disconnects.

macOS

  • Disable IPv6 via System Preferences > Network > Advanced > TCP/IP.
  • Set custom DNS servers provided by your VPN in Network settings.
  • Use third-party apps like Little Snitch to monitor and block unwanted DNS traffic.
  • Ensure the VPN client supports and enables DNS leak protection.

Android

  • Use a VPN app that supports DNS leak protection and IPv6 handling.
  • Disable IPv6 if your device and VPN allow it (may require root on some devices).
  • Configure Private DNS settings (Android 9+) to use your VPN’s DNS or trusted providers.
  • Keep your VPN app updated to benefit from security improvements.

iOS

  • Use VPN apps from reputable providers with built-in DNS leak protection.
  • Disable IPv6 is not straightforward; rely on VPN’s built-in safeguards.
  • Configure DNS settings manually if possible, but iOS limits this flexibility.
  • Use the VPN kill switch feature (available in iOS 14+).

Router

  • Set your router to use VPN-compatible DNS servers to prevent leaks at the network level.
  • Disable IPv6 on the router if your VPN does not support it.
  • Flash custom firmware (e.g., OpenWRT, DD-WRT) that supports advanced VPN and DNS leak protection.
  • Regularly update router firmware to patch vulnerabilities.

Troubleshooting checklist

  1. Verify your VPN is connected and the VPN client is up to date.
  2. Check for DNS leaks using sites like dnsleaktest.com and ipleak.net.
  3. Disable IPv6 on your device if your VPN does not support it properly.
  4. Ensure your VPN client has DNS leak protection enabled in settings.
  5. Configure custom DNS servers supplied by your VPN provider.
  6. Use firewall rules or third-party apps to block DNS requests outside the VPN.
  7. Test your connection on different networks (Wi-Fi, mobile data) to check consistency.
  8. Clear your DNS cache and browser cache to avoid cached leaks.
  9. Disable browser features like WebRTC or use browser extensions to block leaks.
  10. Restart your device and router after applying changes.
  11. Consider switching to a VPN with a strong reputation for leak prevention; see recommendations in the best free VPNs in the UK article.
  12. Review VPN logs and privacy policies to understand data handling; for more, check the no-logs VPN UK guide.

FAQ

What exactly is a DNS leak?

A DNS leak happens when your DNS queries bypass the VPN tunnel and go directly to your ISP or another DNS server, revealing which websites you visit despite using a VPN.

Can IPv6 cause DNS leaks?

Yes, if your VPN does not support IPv6 or does not route IPv6 traffic through the VPN, your device might send IPv6 DNS requests outside the encrypted tunnel, causing leaks.

Does using a free VPN increase the risk of DNS leaks?

Often, yes. Free VPNs may lack comprehensive leak protection and may not properly route DNS requests, increasing the risk of leaks. For a comparison, see free vs paid VPNs in the UK.

How can I check if my VPN has a DNS leak?

You can use online tools like dnsleaktest.com or ipleak.net to run tests that reveal whether your DNS requests are exposed outside the VPN tunnel.

Will disabling IPv6 affect my internet speed or functionality?

Disabling IPv6 might slightly affect performance or prevent access to IPv6-only sites, but it often improves privacy if your VPN does not support IPv6 properly.

Is it possible to completely eliminate DNS leaks?

While it’s challenging to guarantee zero leaks due to various system and network behaviours, configuring your VPN correctly and staying vigilant with testing can minimise leaks significantly.

Does WebRTC cause DNS leaks?

WebRTC can reveal your real IP address even when using a VPN, which is a form of leak. Disabling WebRTC in your browser or using extensions can help prevent this.

Should I be worried about DNS leaks if I only use a VPN for streaming?

Yes, because even streaming activity can reveal your location or ISP if DNS leaks occur. For optimised streaming, see advice in the VPN for BBC iPlayer UK article.
