Using a VPN is one of the most effective methods to safeguard your online privacy and security. However, even the best VPN service can be compromised if it leaks DNS requests, revealing the websites you visit despite the encrypted tunnel. A DNS leak undermines the core purpose of a VPN by exposing sensitive information to your ISP or third parties.

In this article, we will explore what a DNS leak is, why it happens, and how you can detect and fix it to ensure your VPN truly protects your anonymity. Understanding these nuances is crucial for anyone serious about online privacy in 2026 and beyond.

What is a DNS Leak?

Quick answer: A DNS leak occurs when your device sends DNS requests outside the VPN tunnel, revealing your browsing activity to your ISP or other observers.

When you enter a website address, your device uses the Domain Name System (DNS) to translate domain names into IP addresses. Normally, a VPN routes these requests through its own secure DNS servers. However, if DNS requests bypass the VPN and go directly to your ISP’s DNS servers, this is known as a DNS leak.

This leak can expose your browsing history or location, compromising your privacy even when connected to a VPN. DNS leaks are common on some operating systems or with poorly configured VPNs, making it essential to test and address them.

Why Do DNS Leaks Happen?

Quick answer: DNS leaks often result from misconfigured VPN clients, operating system behaviour, or lack of DNS leak protection features.

Several factors can cause DNS leaks, including:

  • Operating system defaults: Some OSes, especially Windows, may prioritise sending DNS requests to configured DNS servers rather than through the VPN tunnel.
  • VPN client limitations: Not all VPN clients force DNS requests through their own servers or may fail to block non-VPN traffic correctly.
  • IPv6 traffic: If your VPN does not support IPv6 or does not route IPv6 DNS requests, leaks may occur.
  • WebRTC leaks: Browser features like WebRTC can expose your true IP address despite the VPN.

Proper configuration and leak protection mechanisms are vital to prevent these issues.

How to Detect DNS Leaks

Quick answer: Use specialised DNS leak test websites and tools to verify if your DNS requests are exposed outside the VPN tunnel.

There are several free tools available online that can help you identify if your DNS requests are leaking. These services show which DNS servers your device is using:

  • DNSLeakTest.com
  • IPLeak.net
  • BrowserLeaks.com (WebRTC test)

When connected to your VPN, run these tests and verify that the DNS servers listed belong to your VPN provider, not your ISP or any third party. If you see your ISP’s DNS servers or unexpected locations, you have a DNS leak.

DNS, IPv6 and WebRTC Leak Tests

Quick answer: Comprehensive leak testing includes DNS, IPv6 and WebRTC tests to ensure no data escapes your VPN’s protection.

While DNS leaks are the most common, IPv6 leaks and WebRTC leaks can also compromise your privacy:

  • IPv6 leaks: Some VPNs only route IPv4 traffic, leaving IPv6 requests unprotected. This can expose your real IP address.
  • WebRTC leaks: WebRTC is a browser feature that can reveal your actual IP address even when connected to a VPN.

Use tools like IPLeak.net or BrowserLeaks.com to perform these tests. If leaks are detected, consider disabling IPv6 on your device and configuring your browser to block WebRTC or use extensions designed for this purpose.

How to Fix a DNS Leak

Quick answer: Fix DNS leaks by using VPNs with built-in leak protection, changing DNS settings, disabling IPv6, and blocking WebRTC in your browser.

Here are some practical steps to fix DNS leaks:

  1. Use a VPN with DNS leak protection: Choose a VPN provider that offers DNS leak protection and routes all DNS requests through their own servers.
  2. Manually configure DNS servers: Change your system DNS to trusted public DNS servers like Cloudflare (1.1.1.1) or Google DNS (8.8.8.8), but only if your VPN supports it.
  3. Disable IPv6: Since many VPNs do not support IPv6, disabling it on your device prevents leaks through this protocol.
  4. Block WebRTC: Use browser settings or extensions to disable WebRTC, which can expose your real IP address despite the VPN.
  5. Enable a kill switch: A VPN kill switch blocks all internet traffic if the VPN disconnects, preventing accidental exposure. Learn more about VPN kill switches here.

Best Practices for Preventing DNS Leaks

Quick answer: Regularly test your VPN for leaks, keep software updated, and use additional privacy tools to maintain security.

To maintain your privacy, consider the following best practices:

  • Regularly check for DNS leaks using the tools mentioned above.
  • Keep your VPN client and device operating system up to date to benefit from security patches.
  • Use privacy-focused browsers and disable unnecessary features that may leak information.
  • Consider combining VPN use with the Tor network for enhanced anonymity.
  • Always enable your VPN’s kill switch to prevent accidental exposure.

Troubleshooting Checklist for DNS Leaks

  1. Confirm you are connected to your VPN before testing for leaks.
  2. Run multiple DNS leak tests from different websites.
  3. Check if your VPN client has DNS leak protection enabled.
  4. Disable IPv6 on your device if your VPN doesn’t support it.
  5. Change your DNS settings to trusted servers if necessary.
  6. Disable or block WebRTC in your browser settings.
  7. Update your VPN client and operating system to the latest versions.
  8. Restart your device and VPN client after making changes.
  9. Consider switching VPN providers if leaks persist.

FAQ

What does a DNS leak mean for my privacy?

A DNS leak means your DNS requests bypass the VPN, exposing your browsing activity and potentially your location to your ISP or other third parties, undermining your privacy.

Can disabling IPv6 completely stop DNS leaks?

Disabling IPv6 can prevent leaks related to that protocol if your VPN does not support IPv6. However, it does not address DNS leaks over IPv4 or WebRTC leaks.

How do I know if my VPN has DNS leak protection?

Check your VPN’s settings or documentation for DNS leak protection features. Many reputable VPNs explicitly mention this feature, or you can test using DNS leak test websites.

Is it safe to use public DNS servers with a VPN?

Using public DNS servers like Google or Cloudflare can improve security if your VPN allows it, but ensure the DNS requests are still routed through the VPN tunnel to avoid leaks.

Why does WebRTC cause leaks even when using a VPN?

WebRTC is a browser technology that can reveal your real IP address directly to websites, bypassing the VPN. Disabling or blocking WebRTC in your browser is essential to prevent this leak.
