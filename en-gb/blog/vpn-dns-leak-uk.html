Using a VPN is a popular way to enhance online privacy and security by encrypting your internet traffic and masking your IP address. However, even with a VPN, your DNS queries—requests that translate domain names into IP addresses—can sometimes bypass the VPN tunnel and leak to your internet service provider (ISP) or other third parties. This phenomenon is known as a DNS leak and can compromise your anonymity.

Understanding when and why DNS leaks occur is essential to maintaining your online privacy. In this guide, we’ll cover how to detect DNS leaks, the common scenarios that cause them, and practical fixes across different platforms in 2026.

Quick answer

  • DNS leaks happen when your DNS requests bypass the VPN tunnel and are sent to your ISP or other untrusted DNS servers.
  • They can expose your browsing activity despite using a VPN, undermining privacy.
  • Testing for leaks involves specialised websites and tools that check DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC leaks.
  • Fixes vary by platform, including configuring VPN apps, adjusting system DNS settings, disabling IPv6, or using a router-level VPN setup.

When does a DNS leak happen?

Key takeaway: A DNS leak occurs when your device sends DNS queries outside the encrypted VPN tunnel, often due to misconfigurations, VPN app limitations, or operating system behaviours. This can inadvertently reveal your true location and browsing activity to your ISP or other observers, even if your IP address appears masked.

Common causes include VPN apps that do not properly route DNS queries through their servers, the presence of IPv6 requests not handled by the VPN, or split-tunnelling settings that allow certain traffic to bypass the VPN. Additionally, some operating systems have default DNS settings that prioritise local or ISP DNS resolvers, which can cause leaks.

Limitations and edge cases

Key takeaway: While many VPN providers advertise DNS leak protection, no solution is infallible. Certain edge cases, such as IPv6 traffic not being supported by the VPN or WebRTC leaks in browsers, can still expose your DNS or IP information. Realistically, maintaining privacy requires ongoing vigilance and combining VPN use with other privacy tools.

For example, some VPNs do not support IPv6, which means DNS queries over IPv6 may leak unless disabled. WebRTC leaks are another vector where browsers can reveal your IP address despite a VPN. Furthermore, free VPN services are more prone to DNS leaks due to limited infrastructure or poor configuration, as discussed in our free vs paid VPN comparison.

How to test DNS/IPv6/WebRTC leaks

Testing for leaks is straightforward using online tools. These services display your visible IP addresses and the DNS servers your device is using. It’s best to test while connected to your VPN to verify that all traffic is properly routed.

Test Type Purpose Recommended Tool
DNS Leak Test Detects if DNS requests go outside VPN tunnel dnsleaktest.com
IPv6 Leak Test Checks if IPv6 traffic bypasses VPN ipleak.net
WebRTC Leak Test Identifies if your browser reveals your real IP via WebRTC browserleaks.com/webrtc

Key takeaway: Regularly testing for DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC leaks helps ensure your VPN is functioning correctly and that your privacy remains intact. Combining these tests with a reliable VPN and proper configuration is the best approach.

Fixes by platform

Windows

  • Use a VPN client with built-in DNS leak protection.
  • Manually configure your network adapter’s DNS settings to use VPN or trusted third-party DNS servers (e.g., Cloudflare 1.1.1.1 or Google 8.8.8.8).
  • Disable IPv6 if your VPN does not support it: Go to Network & Internet settings → Change adapter options → Right-click your connection → Properties → Uncheck Internet Protocol Version 6 (TCP/IPv6).
  • Enable the VPN kill switch to prevent traffic leakage if the VPN connection drops. Learn more in our VPN kill switch guide.

macOS

  • Use a VPN app that includes DNS leak protection and IPv6 handling.
  • Set custom DNS servers in System Preferences → Network → Advanced → DNS tab.
  • Disable IPv6 if necessary via Terminal commands or network settings.
  • Check your browser’s WebRTC settings or use extensions to block WebRTC leaks.

Android

  • Choose a VPN app that supports DNS leak protection and IPv6 handling, such as those reviewed in our VPN for Android guide.
  • Manually set DNS servers in your Wi-Fi advanced settings if your device allows.
  • Consider disabling IPv6 via developer options or using apps that force IPv4-only connections.
  • Avoid free VPNs that may not protect against DNS leaks effectively.

iOS

  • Use a reputable VPN app with built-in DNS leak protection.
  • iOS does not allow manual DNS configuration for cellular connections, so rely on the VPN app’s DNS settings.
  • Disable IPv6 if your VPN supports this option or use a VPN that handles IPv6 properly.
  • Refer to our detailed setup instructions in the VPN iOS guide.

Router

  • Configure your router to use a VPN service, which routes all connected devices through the VPN.
  • Set the router’s DNS servers to trusted ones or those provided by the VPN service.
  • Disable IPv6 on the router if unsupported by your VPN.
  • This method protects all devices on your network but requires compatible router firmware.

Troubleshooting checklist

  1. Verify your VPN app has DNS leak protection enabled.
  2. Run DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC leak tests while connected to the VPN.
  3. Manually configure DNS servers on your device or router to trusted providers.
  4. Disable IPv6 on devices or routers if your VPN does not support it.
  5. Use browser extensions or settings to block WebRTC leaks.
  6. Enable the VPN kill switch to prevent traffic leakage on disconnect.
  7. Update your VPN app and device software regularly.
  8. Avoid free VPNs with poor privacy practices; consider paid options with proven security.
  9. Check for split tunnelling settings that may allow some traffic outside the VPN.
  10. Consider router-level VPN setup for comprehensive network protection.
  11. Clear your DNS cache after making changes to DNS settings.
  12. Consult your VPN provider’s support or knowledge base for platform-specific advice.

FAQ

What is a DNS leak?

A DNS leak happens when your DNS queries are sent outside the encrypted VPN tunnel to your ISP or other DNS servers, revealing your browsing activity despite using a VPN.

How can I check if my VPN has a DNS leak?

You can use websites like dnsleaktest.com or ipleak.net to check for DNS leaks, IPv6 leaks, and WebRTC leaks while connected to your VPN.

Why does IPv6 cause DNS leaks?

Many VPNs do not support IPv6 traffic, so if IPv6 is enabled on your device, DNS requests over IPv6 may bypass the VPN tunnel, causing leaks. Disabling IPv6 or using VPNs with IPv6 support helps prevent this.

Can free VPNs protect against DNS leaks?

Free VPNs often have limited infrastructure and less robust privacy protections, making DNS leaks more likely. For better security, consider reputable paid VPNs, as discussed in our free vs paid VPN comparison.

Is it enough to rely on VPN software alone to prevent leaks?

No. You should also configure your device’s DNS settings, disable IPv6 if unsupported, and test regularly for leaks. Combining these steps provides stronger privacy.

What is WebRTC and how does it cause leaks?

WebRTC is a browser feature that enables real-time communication but can reveal your true IP address even when using a VPN. You can block WebRTC leaks using browser settings or extensions.

Can a router VPN setup prevent DNS leaks?

Yes. Configuring a VPN on your router routes all network traffic through the VPN, reducing the risk of leaks from individual devices. Be sure to set DNS servers and disable IPv6 as needed.
