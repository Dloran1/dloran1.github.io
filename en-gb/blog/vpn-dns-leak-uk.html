When you use a VPN, you expect all your internet traffic to be securely routed and your identity to stay private. But sometimes, a key component of your connection—your DNS requests—can slip through the cracks, revealing more about your browsing habits than you intended. This is called a DNS leak, and it undermines the privacy a VPN promises.

In this article, we'll demystify DNS leaks, show you how to test for them (including other leaks like IPv6 and WebRTC), and walk through practical fixes for every major platform. We'll also discuss realistic privacy limitations and help you decide if you need to change your habits or your technology.

Quick answer: DNS leaks with a VPN

  • If your DNS requests are visible to your ISP or a third party while using a VPN, you have a DNS leak—and your activity may be tracked.
  • DNS leaks often occur due to operating system defaults, VPN app misconfigurations, or mesh network quirks.
  • You can detect leaks using simple online tools and fix most leaks with correct VPN/app/router settings.
  • Advanced leaks (e.g. via WebRTC or IPv6) sometimes require browser or OS-level tweaks beyond the VPN app.
  • No VPN setup is 100% bulletproof—understand privacy limits and check regularly to be safe.
Key takeaway: DNS leaks are unexpected but fixable privacy risks when using a VPN. Regular testing and the right setup can greatly improve your privacy.

When does a DNS leak happen?

A DNS leak occurs whenever your device uses DNS servers outside of your VPN tunnel. This typically happens in a few scenarios:

  • Your device continues to use ISP-provided DNS servers even after a VPN connection is established.
  • Your VPN fails to properly transfer DNS settings or does not enforce DNS leak protection.
  • Operating system or browser functions bypass the VPN or use split tunnelling (e.g. Windows Smart Multi-Homed Name Resolution).
  • Your router or local network settings override VPN DNS configurations.
  • Certain types of internet protocols (like IPv6, DNS over HTTPS) bypass the VPN's routing table.

It's important to note that DNS leaks don't just expose what websites you visit. They could reveal patterns of behaviour, help create tracking profiles, or allow ISPs to censor or redirect traffic.

Key takeaway: DNS leaks happen when requests bypass the VPN—for example, due to OS defaults, router overrides or VPN misconfiguration.

Limitations and edge cases of VPN DNS privacy

VPNs significantly boost privacy, but their DNS leak protection is not foolproof. Here are a few common limitations:

  • VPN app competence: Not all VPN apps implement DNS leak protection to the same standard; some only protect IPv4 DNS, neglecting IPv6.
  • Operating system quirks: Windows, for example, can use parallel DNS resolution, causing DNS requests to be sent to multiple servers at once.
  • Split tunnelling: If enabled, only selected apps pass through the VPN tunnel—others may leak DNS (intentionally or not).
  • Browser behaviour: Modern browsers can send DNS via independent protocols (like DoH - DNS over HTTPS) or WebRTC, leaking info outside the VPN.
  • Routers and mesh networks: Home routers might have hardcoded DNS settings, or override device-level choices, breaking privacy even with a VPN running.

How to test for DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC leaks

Verifying that your VPN is not leaking DNS or other identifiers should be a regular privacy habit. Fortunately, you don’t need to be a network expert; most tests require just a browser.

  1. Connect your VPN.
  2. Visit an online leak test service.
    • Popular sites include dnsleaktest.com, browserleaks.com, and ipleak.net.
  3. Run both basic and extended DNS/IPv6 tests:
    • The results should show only your VPN provider’s DNS servers (not your ISP).
    • Look for any IPv6 address—if you see one from your physical location or ISP, your VPN may not support IPv6 properly.
  4. Test WebRTC leaks:
    • browserleaks.com/webrtc or similar sites will show your real IP if WebRTC is leaking outside the tunnel.
  5. Repeat the process:
    • Test after VPN updates, OS upgrades, or network configuration changes.
    • Always compare the test results with and without the VPN connected.

Key takeaway: Use online tools to check for DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC leaks—test often, as leaks can appear after any update.

How to fix DNS leaks (by platform)

Solving a DNS leak usually involves adjusting your VPN or system network settings. Here’s how it works on all major platforms:

Windows

  • Ensure your VPN is set to enforce DNS leak protection (many have a dedicated setting or toggle).
  • Disable ‘Smart Multi-Homed Name Resolution’ in the Group Policy Editor.
  • Flush your DNS cache after connecting: ipconfig /flushdns in Command Prompt.
  • Set a static DNS server to your VPN provider’s DNS (or public DNS) as a last resort.

macOS

  • Use VPN apps with integrated DNS leak protection, not just system ‘VPN’ services.
  • If leaks persist, configure your network settings to use only VPN-provided DNS.
  • Regularly flush your DNS cache: sudo killall -HUP mDNSResponder in Terminal.

Android

  • Use the VPN app’s built-in DNS protection or set ‘Private DNS’ (found under Network settings) pointing to a trusted server.
  • Avoid VPN ‘Lite’ or proxy modes, which often leak DNS.
  • Watch for aggressive app switching or battery savers disabling VPN background tasks.

iOS

  • Prefer VPN apps with confirmed DNS leak protection (not just iOS’s basic VPN profiles).
  • Use browser apps with DNS over HTTPS (DoH) if possible.
  • Reset network settings if DNS leaks persist after using a VPN app.

Routers

  • Install VPN at router level only if your router supports it natively (OpenVPN or WireGuard builds are best).
  • Manually set DNS servers on your router to those provided by your VPN or another privacy-respecting provider like Cloudflare (1.1.1.1) or NextDNS.
  • Disable ‘smart DNS’, ‘parental controls’ or other value-add features that may override vanilla DNS settings.
  • Restart the router after changes and re-run leak tests from various devices.

If none of these fixes work, ask your VPN provider about their DNS handling or consider using a different VPN app or custom config.

Key takeaway: Fix DNS leaks by forcing your system and router to use VPN-controlled DNS; every platform has a different process.

Troubleshooting checklist: Have you fixed it?

  • Have you tested your connection (DNS, IPv6, WebRTC) while connected to the VPN?
  • After making changes, have you restarted your device and tested again?
  • Did you flush your operating system’s DNS cache?
  • Is your router’s DNS pointing to an appropriate server (VPN, Cloudflare, etc.) and not your ISP?
  • Have you disabled any browser add-on, split tunnelling, parental controls, or network ‘optimisers’ that might override settings?
  • Is your VPN client up-to-date, and does it have DNS leak protection enabled?
  • For mobile: is your VPN still connected when screen is off/after sleep?
  • Browser-specific: have you disabled WebRTC or used a privacy-focused browser for sensitive work?
  • For persistent issues: have you tried with another VPN app?
Key takeaway: Carefully work through the checklist and re-test—leaks often return after system or app updates.

FAQ: DNS leaks and VPN usage

What is a DNS leak in plain English?
It means your device is revealing which websites you visit to someone (usually your ISP), even though you are using a VPN for privacy.
Will any VPN prevent DNS leaks?
No. DNS leak protection is not a given; some VPNs don’t secure DNS by default, especially free or legacy apps.
Can DNS leaks lead to online activity tracking?
Yes. Your DNS queries can reveal your browsing history and potentially your identity, bypassing VPN protection.
Do mobile devices leak DNS more often?
They can, especially if using battery optimisers or VPN ‘Lite’/proxy modes. Always check after OS updates.
What about IPv6 and WebRTC leaks?
These are related but separate: IPv6 and WebRTC can both expose your real IP—even if your DNS is secure.
How can I stop my router from leaking DNS?
Set custom DNS servers (VPN or privacy-focused) in your router settings and disable “Smart DNS” or ISP DNS fallback features.
Is using a public DNS like Google or Cloudflare a fix?
It helps avoid ISP tracking but won't stop all leaks. For best privacy, use your VPN provider’s DNS specifically while VPN is active.
How often should I test for DNS leaks?
At least monthly, and after VPN or OS updates. Any changed configuration warrants a re-test.
Does split tunnelling cause DNS leaks?
It can—apps outside the tunnel may use their own DNS settings. Use with caution for privacy-critical activities.
Can browser extensions leak DNS?
Yes, certain extensions or proxies may override your DNS setup, especially those related to security, privacy, or ad-blocking.
Why does my VPN provider’s DNS show up in my test?
This is normal and means your VPN is handling DNS as expected. Only worry if your ISP’s or local network’s DNS appears instead.
Can I stop leaks without admin/root access?
It's harder, but using trusted VPN apps and browser-level DNS over HTTPS usually help reduce risk.
Key takeaway: Every device, browser, and VPN has quirks—knowing likely questions and answers helps you stay alert to new privacy risks.

Conclusion: Ongoing vigilance required

DNS leaks are one of the most persistent and insidious threats to online privacy, even for VPN users. They aren’t just technical; they are a realistic risk to your browsing secrecy, data integrity, and even your safety in repressive environments. The good news is that most leaks are fixable with careful setup and regular self-audit.

To maximise your privacy:

  • Test often for DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC leaks (especially after software updates).
  • Learn the quirks of your device's operating system and browser.
  • Set up router-level DNS for entire-home privacy if possible.
  • Audit your privacy habits (and app settings) regularly.
  • For advanced scenarios, consider expert guides or professional audits (start with our deep dive on VPN privacy hardening).

Remember: vigilance is key. Privacy is a moving target, and technology changes fast. Stay ahead with ongoing testing and informed choices about your VPN and DNS.
