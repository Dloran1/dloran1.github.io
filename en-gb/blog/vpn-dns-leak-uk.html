Using a VPN is a popular way to enhance online privacy and security by encrypting your internet traffic and masking your IP address. However, even with a VPN, your DNS requests—the queries your device makes to translate website names into IP addresses—can sometimes bypass the VPN tunnel. This is known as a DNS leak, and it can inadvertently expose your browsing activity to your Internet Service Provider (ISP) or other third parties.

In this guide, we’ll explore how DNS leaks occur, how to detect them, and practical fixes across different platforms. While VPNs are effective tools, understanding their limitations and ensuring proper configuration is key to maintaining privacy. For more on VPN basics and their limitations, you might want to check out our article on the disadvantages of VPNs.

Quick answer

  • A DNS leak happens when DNS requests bypass the VPN tunnel and are sent to your ISP’s servers instead of the VPN’s DNS.
  • Leaks can expose your real IP address and browsing history, defeating the purpose of using a VPN.
  • Testing for leaks using online tools is essential to verify your VPN’s effectiveness.
  • Fixes include configuring your device or VPN client to use secure DNS servers and disabling IPv6 or WebRTC leaks.

When does a DNS leak happen?

DNS leaks typically occur when your device sends DNS queries outside the encrypted VPN tunnel, often due to misconfigurations or limitations in the VPN client or operating system. For example, some VPNs may not fully override the DNS settings on your device, or your system may prioritise local DNS servers, causing requests to leak. In other cases, IPv6 or WebRTC protocols can reveal your real IP address despite VPN use.

Key takeaway: A DNS leak occurs when DNS queries bypass the VPN tunnel, potentially exposing your true IP address and browsing behaviour to your ISP or other observers.

Limitations and edge cases

Not all VPNs handle DNS traffic equally, and some free or poorly configured VPNs may be more prone to leaks. Additionally, certain network environments, such as corporate or public Wi-Fi, can interfere with VPN DNS settings. IPv6 leaks are a common edge case because many VPNs focus primarily on IPv4 traffic. WebRTC leaks, caused by browser features, can also reveal your IP address even if DNS is secure. It’s important to understand that no VPN can guarantee perfect anonymity; DNS leak prevention is just one part of a broader privacy strategy.

Key takeaway: VPNs have varying effectiveness against DNS leaks, and edge cases like IPv6 and WebRTC leaks require extra attention and specific mitigation steps.

How to test DNS/IPv6/WebRTC leaks

Testing your VPN for leaks is straightforward and should be done regularly to ensure your privacy remains intact. Several online tools provide comprehensive leak testing, checking DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC vulnerabilities. Below is a comparison of some popular testing websites:

Key takeaway: Regularly use trusted online testing tools to verify your VPN does not leak DNS, IPv6, or WebRTC data, as these leaks can silently compromise your privacy.

Fixes by platform

Windows

  • Disable IPv6 in Network Adapter settings: Open Network Connections > right-click your VPN adapter > Properties > uncheck Internet Protocol Version 6.
  • Manually configure DNS servers to use trusted options like Google DNS (8.8.8.8, 8.8.4.4) or Cloudflare (1.1.1.1).
  • Enable the VPN client’s built-in DNS leak protection feature, if available.
  • Consider using a VPN client that supports a kill switch to prevent traffic outside the VPN.
  • Flush DNS cache: Open Command Prompt and run ipconfig /flushdns after changes.

macOS

  • Disable IPv6 via System Preferences > Network > Advanced > TCP/IP > Configure IPv6: set to “Link-local only” or “Off”.
  • Set DNS servers manually to secure options like Cloudflare or OpenDNS.
  • Use VPN apps with built-in leak protection and kill switch functionality.
  • Clear DNS cache by running sudo killall -HUP mDNSResponder in Terminal.

Android

  • Use VPN apps that explicitly mention DNS leak protection.
  • Disable IPv6 if the VPN app supports it or through developer options (may not be available on all devices).
  • Manually configure private DNS under Settings > Network & Internet > Private DNS to use a provider like dns.google or 1dot1dot1dot1.cloudflare-dns.com.
  • Keep the VPN app updated and avoid free VPNs without leak protection (see our free vs paid VPN comparison).

iOS

  • Use VPN apps from reputable providers with DNS leak protection.
  • Disable IPv6 is not straightforward on iOS; rely on VPN client’s built-in protections.
  • Set DNS servers manually in Wi-Fi settings by tapping the “i” icon and configuring DNS to manual with secure servers.
  • Regularly check for leaks using online tools and update your VPN app.
  • For detailed setup, visit our VPN on iOS guide.

Router

  • Configure your router to use VPN-compatible firmware (e.g., DD-WRT, OpenWRT) that supports DNS leak protection.
  • Set your router’s DNS servers to trusted providers, avoiding ISP DNS.
  • Disable IPv6 on your router if your VPN does not support it or leaks it.
  • Ensure the VPN client on your router has DNS leak protection and a kill switch.
  • Consult your router’s manual or support forums for VPN-specific DNS leak fixes.

Troubleshooting checklist

  1. Verify your VPN is connected properly before testing for leaks.
  2. Run DNS leak tests at dnsleaktest.com or ipleak.net.
  3. Disable IPv6 on your device or router if your VPN does not support it.
  4. Check your VPN client settings for DNS leak protection and enable it.
  5. Manually set your DNS servers to secure options like Cloudflare (1.1.1.1) or Google DNS (8.8.8.8).
  6. Flush your DNS cache after making configuration changes.
  7. Disable WebRTC in your browser or use extensions that block it.
  8. Update your VPN app and device OS regularly.
  9. Restart your device and router after applying changes.
  10. Consider switching to a VPN provider with a strong reputation for leak protection (see our guide on the best free VPNs in the UK for trustworthy options).
  11. Test leaks on different networks to rule out network-specific issues.
  12. Consult your VPN provider’s support for advanced troubleshooting.

FAQ

What exactly is a DNS leak?

A DNS leak occurs when your DNS requests bypass the VPN tunnel and are sent directly to your ISP or another DNS provider, potentially exposing your browsing activity and real IP address despite being connected to a VPN.

How can I check if my VPN is leaking DNS?

You can use online tools such as DNSLeakTest.com or IPLeak.net to run tests that identify whether your DNS requests are leaking outside the VPN tunnel.

Does disabling IPv6 fix DNS leaks?

Disabling IPv6 often helps prevent leaks because many VPNs only route IPv4 traffic through the VPN tunnel. If IPv6 traffic is not handled properly, it can leak your real IP address and DNS queries.

Can my browser cause DNS leaks even if my VPN is working?

Yes. Features like WebRTC in browsers can reveal your real IP address independently of DNS queries. You can disable WebRTC or use browser extensions to block such leaks.

Are free VPNs more likely to have DNS leaks?

Often, yes. Many free VPNs lack robust DNS leak protection and may use your ISP’s DNS servers by default. For a comparison of free and paid VPNs, see our article on free vs paid VPNs in the UK.

What is the best DNS server to use with a VPN?

Trusted DNS providers like Cloudflare (1.1.1.1), Google DNS (8.8.8.8), and OpenDNS are popular choices. Some VPN providers offer their own DNS servers designed to prevent leaks and improve privacy.

Does a DNS leak mean my VPN is useless?

Not necessarily, but it does mean your privacy is compromised in that aspect. DNS leaks can reveal your browsing destinations, so it’s important to fix them to maintain the anonymity benefits your VPN provides.

Can I prevent DNS leaks on my router?

Yes, by configuring your router to use VPN-compatible firmware with DNS leak protection, setting secure DNS servers, and disabling IPv6 if necessary. This protects all devices on your network without configuring each one individually.
