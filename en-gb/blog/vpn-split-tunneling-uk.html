What split tunnelling is (and isn’t)

By default, a VPN app sends all traffic through an encrypted tunnel. With split tunnelling you define exceptions: selected apps or destinations bypass the tunnel and use your regular connection. The opposite mode — “inverse split” — routes only chosen apps via VPN and lets everything else go direct. Split tunnelling is not a firewall, QoS or ad-blocker; it only decides which interface the traffic uses.

Rule #1: keep browsers, messengers and P2P inside the tunnel. Exclude only low-risk utilities that genuinely break behind VPN exits.

App vs URL vs Inverse split

  • App-based: choose apps that bypass VPN (banking, SSO launchers, printer/NAS tools). Easiest and safest.
  • URL/domain-based: allow-list specific hostnames (e.g. corporate SSO, printer portals). Requires providers that support domain rules or a router with policy routing.
  • Inverse split: only nominated apps use VPN (e.g., torrent client + browser), everything else goes direct. Useful on low-powered routers or workstations with strict LAN needs.

When to use it (UK use-cases)

Good candidates to bypass VPN: UK banking helper apps, corporate SSO agents (Citrix/Okta), printer/scanner utilities, NAS/SMB clients, smart-home dashboards, AirPrint/Bonjour discovery.
Keep in VPN: browsers (Chrome/Edge/Firefox), streaming apps (iPlayer, Netflix), email/messengers, P2P clients and anything handling personal data.

If your goal is whole-home coverage (Apple TV, consoles, Smart TVs), consider a router VPN setup and apply split at the router with policy routing. It’s cleaner and avoids per-device quirks.

Windows — exact steps

  1. Open your VPN app → Settings → Split tunnelling (naming varies).
  2. Choose Bypass VPN for selected apps and add:
    • Banking app/site helper, SSO launchers.
    • Printer/scanner tools, NAS clients.
  3. Ensure Use provider DNS is ON in the VPN app.
  4. Restart the apps you changed; for browsers press Alt+F4 to fully exit and relaunch.

Performance tip: prefer WireGuard/NordLynx. Switch to OpenVPN TCP/443 on restrictive Wi-Fi. Full guide: VPN for Windows.

macOS — exact steps

Consumer clients on macOS typically split by app. If you need domain-level control (e.g., let *.company.com go direct), either use a provider with URL rules or move the split to the router. Always keep the macOS firewall on and allow the VPN helper extensions.

  • Exclude: printer/NAS utilities, banking helpers, AirPrint discovery.
  • Keep in VPN: Safari/Chrome/Firefox, streaming apps, P2P clients.
  • After changes, flush DNS: sudo dscacheutil -flushcache; sudo killall -HUP mDNSResponder

See also: macOS VPN guide.

Android — exact steps

  1. In the VPN app → Split tunnelling → Exclude apps.
  2. Add: UK banking, printer utilities, smart-home controllers. Keep browsers/streaming inside VPN.
  3. Enable Always-on and Block connections without VPN if your app supports a true kill switch.

Router policies (whole-home split)

For Apple TV, consoles and Smart TVs the cleanest approach is policy routing on the router: devices or subnets that should use VPN are placed into the tunnel; others go out direct. Benefits: one place to maintain rules, consistent DNS, no per-device split quirks.

Avoiding DNS/WebRTC/IPv6 leaks

  • Provider DNS: keep it enabled in the VPN app; avoid external DoH/DoT outside the tunnel.
  • WebRTC: disable local IP exposure in the browser settings or extensions.
  • IPv6: if your provider doesn’t tunnel IPv6, disable IPv6 temporarily to avoid partial leaks.
  • After changes: reconnect VPN and relaunch affected apps; clear app caches if streaming errors persist.

BBC iPlayer / Netflix reliability

  • Keep the streaming app inside the tunnel; bypassing it causes IP↔DNS mismatches.
  • On proxy/unblocker errors: rotate UK server → fully quit app (⌘Q/Alt+F4) → relaunch. On strict networks use OpenVPN TCP/443.
  • After changing country, relaunch apps to refresh the route.
