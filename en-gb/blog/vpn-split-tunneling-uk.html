VPN Split Tunnelling (UK, 2025) — What to Exclude, How to Set Up & How to Avoid Streaming Errors

Updated: 2025-09-25 • ~20–24 min read

Split tunnelling lets you route selected apps or domains outside the VPN while everything else stays encrypted. Done right, it fixes UK banking logins and SCA, corporate SSO, printer/NAS discovery and smart-home dashboards — without sacrificing privacy for browsers, streaming or P2P. This guide gives precise Windows/macOS/Android rules, whole-home router policies, a leak-free checklist and a practical troubleshooting matrix.

What split tunnelling is

By default, a VPN app sends all device traffic through the encrypted tunnel. Split tunnelling introduces exceptions: selected apps or destinations (domains/URLs) can go direct, outside the tunnel. The opposite mode (inverse split) routes only a few apps via VPN while everything else goes direct. This is not a firewall, QoS or ad-blocker — it only decides which path traffic takes.

Practically you have two “pipes”: the VPN pipe and the direct pipe. Sloppy rules (e.g., letting your browser or torrent client bypass the tunnel) cause privacy loss and IP↔DNS conflicts. Precise rules fix problematic utilities without compromising protection for the rest.

Rule #1: keep browsers, messengers, email and P2P inside the tunnel. Exclude only low-risk helpers that genuinely fail behind VPN exits.

Modes: apps, domains, inverse

  • App-based: the simplest and safest. Exclude a banking helper, SSO agent, printer/NAS utilities.
  • Domain/URL-based: allow-lists (e.g., *.company.com or a printer portal). Requires app support or router rules (policy routing).
  • Inverse split: only selected apps (e.g., browser + P2P client) go through VPN; the rest goes direct. Useful on low-power routers or LAN-sensitive workstations.

Most consumer clients provide app-based split on Windows and Android. On macOS, domain rules are rarer — the cleanest place for them is the router.

Benefits & trade-offs

Pros: banking/SCA works, you don’t break printers/NAS discovery, consoles and Apple TV are easier when the VPN runs on a router, and latency drops for apps that don’t need VPN.
Cons: misconfiguration can leak browser/P2P, or break streaming when DNS and app traffic take different paths. More moving parts to debug.

When to use it (UK scenarios)

  • Banking & SCA: some banks distrust VPN ranges; exclude the helper while keeping the browser in the tunnel.
  • Corporate SSO/legacy portals: Citrix/Okta/SharePoint may be region-locked or dislike exit IPs. Exclude the SSO agent; keep work browsers in VPN if policy allows.
  • LAN discovery: printers (AirPrint/Bonjour), scanners and NAS need local broadcasts. Exclude their utilities; keep everything else protected.
  • Smart-home: local dashboards and auto-discovery can be finicky — exclude the controller app if necessary.

If the goal is whole-home protection (Apple TV, consoles, TVs), move the logic to the router — see VPN on a router and UK router setup.

Windows — steps, verification & presets

  1. In your VPN app → Split tunnelling → “Bypass VPN for selected apps”.
  2. Add: banking helper/site launcher, SSO agent (Citrix/Okta), printer/scanner utilities, NAS client.
  3. Enable Use provider DNS. If your browser uses third-party DoH/DoT, disable it or ensure it rides via the VPN interface.
  4. Fully quit and relaunch affected apps (browser: Alt+F4).

Verification (2 minutes)

  1. With the browser (kept in VPN), open an IP/DNS leak test (see guide). DNS should show your VPN provider, not ISP.
  2. Launch the excluded utility (e.g., printer/NAS). If it works while the browser still shows VPN DNS — your split is correct.
  • Protocol: WireGuard/NordLynx for throughput and low CPU; on restrictive networks use OpenVPN TCP/443.
  • Endpoints: rotate to lower-load UK nodes (London/Manchester/Birmingham), especially at evening peaks.

macOS — steps, verification & presets

On macOS, split is typically per-app. If you need domain rules (e.g., *.company.com), use a client with URL allow-lists or move the logic to the router. Keep the macOS firewall on and allow VPN network extensions.

  • Exclude: printer/NAS helpers, banking helpers, AirPrint/Bonjour discovery.
  • Keep in VPN: Safari/Chrome/Firefox, streaming apps, P2P, email, messengers.
  • After changes flush DNS: sudo dscacheutil -flushcache; sudo killall -HUP mDNSResponder

Verification: repeat the IP/DNS test as on Windows and confirm the excluded LAN app works in parallel.

Android — steps, verification & presets

  1. In the VPN app → Split tunnelling → Exclude apps.
  2. Add: banking, printer utilities, smart-home controllers. Keep browsers, streaming and P2P inside the tunnel.
  3. Enable Always-on and Block connections without VPN (true kill switch) if supported.
  • Battery: exclude the VPN app from battery optimisations so Android doesn’t kill the tunnel in background.
  • Protocol: WireGuard for efficiency; try IKEv2 if Wi-Fi/LTE roaming feels choppy.

Verification: run an IP/DNS test in the browser (should show VPN DNS) while the excluded app (e.g., printer panel) still reaches LAN resources.

Router policies (whole-home)

The cleanest place for split is the router (policy routing): you nominate devices/VLANs that must use VPN; everything else goes out via WAN. Benefits: consistent DNS, one place to maintain rules, no per-device quirks.

Mini how-to (policy routing in 4 steps)

  1. Device map: assign static IP/MAC (DHCP reservations) for Apple TV, consoles, laptops.
  2. Groups/policies: create “Via VPN” (e.g., TV VLAN) and “Direct” (IoT, consoles) policies.
  3. DNS: devices in the VPN policy should use provider DNS through the tunnel; otherwise expect IP↔DNS mismatches.
  4. Reboot: after saving rules, restart media devices (TV/Apple TV) to refresh routes and peering.

Performance & practical tips

  • Protocol: WireGuard/NordLynx for maximum throughput; OpenVPN TCP/443 as Plan B on hotel/office networks.
  • Wi-Fi: prefer 5 GHz or Ethernet, especially for 4K.
  • Endpoint load: rotate to lower-load UK nodes in the evening; keep 2–3 favourites.
  • Background sync: pause heavy cloud sync (OneDrive/iCloud) during gaming/streaming.

Streaming stability (BBC iPlayer/Netflix)

The most common issue is an IP↔DNS conflict: the streaming app travels one path while DNS resolves on another. Keep streaming apps inside the tunnel, enforce provider DNS, and on errors rotate servers and relaunch the app.

Avoiding DNS/WebRTC/IPv6 leaks

  • Provider DNS: keep it enabled; avoid external DoH/DoT outside the tunnel. If you must use it, force it through the VPN interface.
  • WebRTC: disable local IP exposure in browser settings or via an extension. Restart the browser after rule changes.
  • IPv6: if your provider doesn’t tunnel IPv6, temporarily disable IPv6 on OS/router to avoid partial leaks.
  • Verification: reconnect the VPN and run an IP/DNS test; re-test after client or OS updates.

FAQ

Is split tunnelling safe?

Yes — if you only exclude low-risk utilities (banking helpers, printer/NAS) and keep browsers, streaming and P2P inside the tunnel. Enforce provider DNS and re-run IP/DNS tests after changes.

App-based vs domain-based split?

App-based is simpler and safer — you’re less likely to leak browser traffic. Domain-based offers precision but can accidentally catch CDNs; best implemented on the router.

WireGuard or OpenVPN for split?

WireGuard (NordLynx) generally offers better throughput and lower CPU/battery use. OpenVPN TCP/443 helps on aggressive hotel/office networks because it blends with HTTPS.

How do I test for leaks quickly?

1) Connect VPN 2) Open an IP/DNS leak test (see the guide) 3) DNS should be your VPN provider 4) Launch the excluded LAN app and confirm the browser’s DNS result stays on VPN.

Is split OK for compliance (work/UK GDPR)?

Depends on company policy. If full-tunnel is required, use a separate profile or router VLAN policies and avoid device-level split on the work machine.

When should I avoid split entirely?

On devices processing sensitive work data, or wherever policy mandates a full tunnel. Keep everything in VPN and separate traffic at the router instead.
