Before you start (clean slate)

Old profiles and DNS configs cause the majority of iOS VPN issues. Spend two minutes to reset things properly:

  • Remove legacy VPN profiles: Settings → General → VPN & Device Management → delete profiles from old providers.
  • Delete custom DNS profiles: Settings → General → VPN & Device Management → Configuration Profiles and remove any “DNS/DoH” profiles unless supplied by your VPN and routed through the tunnel.
  • Reset network settings (last resort): if you’ve experimented with many profiles, consider Settings → General → Transfer or Reset iPhone → Reset → Reset Network Settings (you’ll re-enter Wi-Fi passwords).
  • Update iOS and the VPN app: bug fixes often target VPN networking and power management.

A clean baseline avoids IP↔DNS mismatches that cause iPlayer/Netflix proxy errors and “some sites don’t load” issues.

Quick setup (clean & reliable)

  1. Install your provider’s iOS app from the UK App Store.
  2. Sign in → allow VPN configurations and Local Network when prompted.
  3. Open the app’s protocol settings and pick WireGuard/NordLynx (fastest) or IKEv2 (smooth handover on Wi-Fi ⇄ 4G/5G).
  4. Enable Use provider DNS. Avoid external “Secure DNS”/DoH profiles unless you are sure they route via the tunnel.
  5. Pick a UK endpoint (London/Manchester/Birmingham) and save 2–3 favourites for peak times. Consider adding an EU fallback (Dublin/Amsterdam).
  6. Turn on Auto-connect and the app’s Kill Switch. For IKEv2, ensure Connect On Demand is enabled in the profile.
  7. Test: reconnect VPN → run an IP/DNS leak test → launch BBC iPlayer or Netflix and confirm there’s no proxy warning.

Per-app split tunnelling: iOS consumer apps don’t support it. If you need app/domain rules, use a router with policy routing.

iCloud Private Relay, DoH & Safari

Apple features that “help with privacy” can clash with your VPN path if misconfigured. Keep it simple:

  • Disable iCloud Private Relay for the connection you use with the VPN (Settings → Apple ID → iCloud → Private Relay).
  • If enabled, turn off “Limit IP address tracking” in the Wi-Fi network details for the SSID you use with the VPN.
  • Secure DNS/DoH profiles: avoid installing third-party DoH unless your provider instructs you and routes it via the tunnel.
  • Safari: it already minimises WebRTC local IP exposure. For third-party browsers, set WebRTC to “public IP only” if the option exists.

Mixed paths (e.g., DNS via Apple/DoH, traffic via VPN) are the #1 reason for geo/streaming errors.

On-Demand, Kill Switch & Captive Portals

  • Auto-connect handles daily use. For IKEv2, ensure Connect On Demand keeps sessions alive after sleep/roaming.
  • Captive portals: when joining hotel/café Wi-Fi, disconnect the VPN, open the splash page, complete the login, then reconnect the VPN. Some apps include a “Detect Captive Portal” toggle — keep it on while travelling.
  • Local network access: grant “Local Network” permission to the VPN app so AirPrint/Chromecast discovery works; it doesn’t bypass the VPN — it allows local broadcasts.
  • Home/office SSIDs: consider separate SSIDs: Home-VPN (phones/laptops via VPN) and Home-Direct (consoles/IoT). See router VPN.

Avoiding IP/DNS/WebRTC/IPv6 leaks

  • Provider DNS only: confirm the app uses its resolver through the tunnel. Reconnect after toggling options.
  • IPv6: if your provider doesn’t tunnel IPv6, prefer one that does, or disable IPv6 on the VPN SSID at the router to avoid partial leaks.
  • WebRTC: Safari is conservative; on Chromium-based browsers, limit WebRTC local IPs where offered.
  • Verify regularly: reconnect → test IP/DNS → relaunch streaming apps. Re-test after iOS/app updates.

BBC iPlayer / Netflix reliability

A stable, single path is key. iOS can’t split a single app’s traffic in consumer mode, so keep all of the streaming app inside the tunnel:

  • On error: rotate to another UK server → fully quit the app (swipe away) → relaunch. On strict Wi-Fi, try OpenVPN TCP/443.
  • After changing city/country, restart the app/TV stick to refresh peering/DNS caches.
  • Use provider DNS; external DoH will break region checks even if your IP is UK.

Battery, roaming & travel presets

  • Protocol choice: WireGuard is the most efficient. IKEv2 is second best, especially when bouncing between Wi-Fi and 4G/5G.
  • Background App Refresh: allow it for your VPN app so iOS doesn’t suspend the tunnel on sleep.
  • Low Power Mode: may delay reconnects. Disable it during long sessions if you notice drops.
  • Roaming preset: save two UK favourites and one nearby EU endpoint (Dublin/Amsterdam) to survive hotel Wi-Fi oddities.
  • Wi-Fi Assist & Private Address: “Private Wi-Fi Address” can confuse allow-lists tied to MAC. If you use MAC-based rules at home, keep it off for your home SSID.
  • eSIM + hotspot: if tethering an iPad from an iPhone, run the VPN on the tethering device for simpler paths.

iPhone with a router VPN (policies)

If your home uses a router VPN, keep iPhones on the VPN SSID and guests on a Direct SSID. Policy routing prevents DNS conflicts and keeps streaming consistent on TVs, while your phone uses the same UK resolver as the TV.

Typical split: Work laptop/iPhone → VPN SSID • Consoles/IoT → Direct • Smart TV/Apple TV → VPN SSID.

Shortcuts & Focus automations

While iOS doesn’t expose per-app split tunnelling, you can still automate when the VPN connects:

  • Shortcuts → Automations: create automations “When I open Streaming folder” → Open app → then Open VPN app (most providers deep-link to “connect”).
  • Focus modes: set a “Travel” Focus that triggers your VPN app and disables Private Relay/Limit IP Tracking on known SSIDs.
  • Widget: add your provider’s connect/disconnect widget to the Home Screen for one-tap control.

Advanced: MTU & strict networks

On some hotel/campus networks, you’ll see video buffering or sites half-loading every few seconds. That can be an MTU issue.

  • Try a lower MTU on the VPN (if the app allows) or on your travel router (typical sweet spot 1380–1420 for UDP tunnelling).
  • Fallback to TCP/443 when UDP is throttled; it rides over HTTPS rules but costs some battery/latency.
  • Disable HTTP/3 (QUIC) in apps that allow it (e.g., some browsers) if the network mangles UDP/443.

FAQ — quick answers

Does iOS support per-app split tunnelling?
Not in consumer apps. Per-app VPN exists in MDM/enterprise. For home use, do policies on the router.
Private Relay vs VPN — can I use both?
No. Private Relay conflicts with the VPN path and DNS. Turn it off while using a VPN.
Which protocol should I pick on iPhone?
WireGuard (or NordLynx) for speed/battery; IKEv2 for roaming; OpenVPN TCP/443 only for strict networks.
How do I know I’m leak-free?
Reconnect → run an IP/DNS test (should show your VPN provider) → launch iPlayer/Netflix with no proxy warning.
Is a Dedicated IP better on iOS?
It reduces banking flags but isn’t a streaming silver bullet. See Dedicated IP (UK).
Can I automate the VPN on iOS?
Yes. Use Shortcuts automations (e.g., when opening a Streaming folder) to launch your VPN app, and Focus modes to ensure it connects on travel SSIDs.
Should I disable IPv6 on iOS?
Prefer providers that tunnel IPv6. If not available, disable IPv6 on the VPN SSID at the router to avoid partial leaks.
