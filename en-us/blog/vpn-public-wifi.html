Using a VPN on Public Wi-Fi: Stay Safe at Starbucks, Airports and Hotels

Quick answer: Public Wi-Fi is risky. A VPN encrypts your traffic and prevents snooping, but only if DNS/IPv6/WebRTC are configured correctly.

Think of public Wi-Fi like a crowded room. Without a VPN, anyone close enough can listen. With a VPN, your conversation is locked inside an encrypted tunnel.

Why Public Wi-Fi Is Dangerous (US Reality)

In the US, free Wi-Fi is everywhere: Starbucks, McDonald’s, airports like JFK or LAX, hotels and public libraries. Convenience comes at a cost. Many hotspots lack proper isolation, making it trivial to sniff traffic or create fake “Evil Twin” networks.

Traffic on public Wi-Fi without a VPN

Common attacks: packet sniffing, session hijacking, DNS manipulation and Evil Twin hotspots.

How a VPN Changes the Picture

Traffic with a VPN tunnel

A VPN encrypts traffic end-to-end. Even if someone captures packets, they see gibberish.

Personal test note: While testing public Wi-Fi at a US airport lounge, I saw DNS queries leak outside the tunnel until I enabled “Use VPN DNS” and the kill switch. After that, all requests resolved via the VPN.

How to Test Your VPN on Public Wi-Fi

Run these checks every time you connect to a new hotspot:

Fixes by Platform

  • Windows: enable kill switch, disable IPv6.
  • macOS: prefer IKEv2/WireGuard.
  • iOS: always-on VPN.
  • Android: block connections without VPN.
  • Router: protect all devices.

Conclusion

Public Wi-Fi in the US is convenient but risky. A VPN dramatically improves safety — when configured correctly. Test for leaks, use a kill switch, and choose providers with a strict no-logs policy.
