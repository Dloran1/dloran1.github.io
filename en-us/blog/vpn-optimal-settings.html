Quick summary: For most US users, the best baseline is: WireGuard/NordLynx protocol → Auto (nearest/least-loaded) server → Kill Switch ON → DNS & IPv6 leak protection ON → Auto-connect ON → Split tunneling OFF. Fine-tune only for specific goals like streaming foreign libraries, P2P seeding, gaming latency or bypassing restrictive networks.

1) Pick the right protocol (speed vs. resilience)

Start with WireGuard (or vendor variant like NordLynx). It delivers the best throughput and handshake times in most US regions. If a network filters UDP or blocks modern ciphers, fall back to OpenVPN UDP for balance or OpenVPN TCP for reliability. Legacy options (PPTP/L2TP/IPsec) shouldn’t be used in 2025.

Need a deeper comparison before you commit? See our primer VPN protocols explained.

2) Server selection: automatic first, then manual low-ping picks

Start with the app’s Auto/Quick Connect for a fast baseline. Then open the server list and sort by latency/load. US networks vary a lot by coast and peering: if New York feels loaded in the evening, try Washington DC or even Montreal; on the West Coast, compare Los Angeles vs. San Jose/Seattle. If your goal is a specific platform abroad, combine this with the tips from Which VPN server should I choose?.

3) Make it fail-safe: Kill Switch & Auto-connect

Enable the Kill Switch so traffic can’t leak if the tunnel drops on sleep/wake or Wi-Fi roam. Then enable Auto-connect on unknown/public networks, and on system start for laptops. These two switches close 90% of accidental leak scenarios in cafés, hotels and airports.

4) Leak protection: DNS, IPv6 and WebRTC

Turn on in-app DNS leak protection and provider DNS. If available, enable IPv6 blocking (many US ISPs route IPv6 separately). In browsers, limit WebRTC local IP exposure. To verify your setup, follow our checks in DNS leak: test & fix.

5) Split tunneling (use only when necessary)

Keep split tunneling OFF by default — every exemption is a potential IP/DNS leak path. Add exceptions only for apps that truly require it (some banking apps, local printers/NAS). For remote teams and hybrid setups, review safe patterns in VPN for remote work.

6) Streaming & geo-access

Use streaming-optimized servers when available and reconnect to refresh your exit IP if a service buffers or prompts verification. Some platforms vary by time of day; switch to the provider-recommended region and test again. For speed benchmarking across candidates, follow our checklist in VPN speed test.

7) Port rules, NAT & local network access

You don’t need port forwarding unless you expect inbound traffic (seedboxes, self-hosted services, remote desktop). If you enable it, restrict the scope and understand the exposure. We break down practical do’s and don’ts in VPN port forwarding.

8) Public Wi-Fi profile (US travel)

On hotel/airport Wi-Fi, keep Kill Switch + Auto-connect ON, firewall ON, and disable file/printer sharing. For a condensed checklist (Android/iOS/macOS/Windows), use VPN for public Wi-Fi.

Tip: If speeds drop unexpectedly, rotate the server city, flip UDP⇄TCP, and clear the app’s DNS cache. Most issues resolve with those three moves.

FAQ — Optimal VPN Settings (US)

Will these settings slow my internet?

Not much. With WireGuard/NordLynx and a nearby server, overhead is usually under 10% on modern broadband.

What’s the best VPN protocol in 2025?

WireGuard/NordLynx for most users. If blocked, fall back to OpenVPN UDP; use TCP for restrictive or corporate networks.

How do I prevent DNS or IPv6 leaks?

Enable the app’s DNS leak protection and IPv6 blocking; then run a quick check using our DNS leak guide.

Do I need special settings on mobile?

Yes: enable auto-connect on Wi-Fi & mobile data, prefer WireGuard/IKEv2, keep the kill switch on.

What are the recommended defaults?

Protocol: WireGuard/NordLynx • Server: Auto (nearest/least-loaded) • Kill Switch: ON • DNS/IPv6: ON • Auto-connect: ON • Split tunneling: OFF.
