VPNs are trusted for their promise of privacy, but even while connected, your real online activity may still be exposed through DNS leaks. These leaks can undermine what a VPN is supposed to provide: keeping your internet requests hidden from your internet service provider (ISP) and potential snoopers.

Understanding DNS leaks, knowing how to test for them, and learning practical ways to fix them across devices are crucial skills for anyone serious about digital privacy in 2026. This article cuts through jargon, focusing on actionable steps and realistic limitations of modern VPN setups.

Quick answer: DNS Leaks with a VPN

  • DNS leaks occur when your device sends website requests outside the VPN tunnel, exposing your real ISP.
  • You can check for leaks using web-based DNS leak testing tools and device settings.
  • Common fixes include enabling VPN's DNS leak protection, disabling IPv6, and configuring custom DNS servers.
  • Windows and public Wi-Fi networks are especially vulnerable to leaks without proper VPN setup.
  • No solution is perfect—some edge cases and technical limits remain even in 2026.
Key takeaway: DNS leaks can occur unexpectedly, but regular testing and applying device-specific fixes offer strong privacy protection for most users.

When and Why DNS Leaks Happen

DNS leaks can occur any time your device bypasses the VPN tunnel for Domain Name System (DNS) requests. Here's a breakdown of common scenarios, why they happen, and the risks:

Scenario Why It Happens Privacy Risk
VPN Disconnects Unexpectedly Failsafe (kill switch) not enabled, so traffic reverts to your real ISP DNS servers Your true IP and DNS queries are exposed
Incorrect VPN Configuration VPN app doesn't set or enforce its own DNS servers System uses ISP DNS alongside or instead of VPN DNS
IPv6 Support Not Handled VPN only tunnels IPv4; leaks occur via IPv6 ISP sees your browsing via IPv6 DNS requests
WebRTC Browser Features WebRTC bypasses VPN for peer connections Real IP or DNS details leak to sites
Split Tunneling Misuse Apps/browsers excluded from VPN tunnel Those apps leak real DNS info
Key takeaway: DNS leaks often result from VPN disconnects, poor configuration, or unhandled IPv6 and WebRTC traffic.

Limitations and Edge Cases: Why Leaks Still Happen in 2026

Despite improvements by VPN providers, no setup is 100% foolproof. Here are key limitations to be aware of:

  • Public Wi-Fi Networks: Some public hotspots forcibly redirect DNS requests, bypassing your VPN’s DNS.
  • OS Updates: Systems like Windows may reset DNS settings after updates, re-enabling IPv6.
  • Browser Behavior: Features like DNS over HTTPS sometimes conflict with VPN routing.
  • IoT Devices: Smart TVs may have hard-coded DNS that bypasses router-level VPNs.
Key takeaway: OS updates, browser features, and IoT devices can introduce leaks even with a VPN active.

How to Test for Leaks: DNS, IPv6, and WebRTC

Testing for privacy leaks is essential. Here’s how to do it:

  • DNS Leak: Visit a DNS leak test site. If your ISP’s name appears, you have a leak.
  • IPv6 Leak: Use an IPv6 leak testing tool. If your real IPv6 address shows, it's not being tunneled.
  • WebRTC Leak: Run a WebRTC test. If your real local IP appears, your browser is leaking.
Key takeaway: Use both online tools and system-level checks (like ipconfig) to identify persistent leaks.

Fixes by Platform: Windows, macOS, Android, iOS, and Routers

Windows

  • Enable “DNS leak protection” and “kill switch” in your VPN app.
  • Flush DNS cache: ipconfig /flushdns.
  • Disable IPv6 if your VPN doesn't support it.

macOS

  • Check "Send all traffic over VPN connection" in settings.
  • Set IPv6 to "Link-local only" to prevent leaks.

Android & iOS

  • Use private DNS settings (Android 12+) or custom DNS profiles (iOS).
  • Disable split-tunneling for sensitive apps.

Routers

  • Set DNS manually at the router level and block outgoing Port 53 except via VPN.

Troubleshooting Checklist

  • Restart your device after changing DNS settings.
  • Disable "Smart Multi-Homing" features on Windows.
  • Clear browser cache and test in Incognito mode.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is a DNS leak?
It’s when your device sends website address lookups outside the VPN tunnel, revealing your activity to your ISP.
Does a kill switch help?
Yes, it blocks traffic if the VPN drops, preventing accidental leaks during connection failures.
Are WebRTC leaks the same?
No, WebRTC leaks expose your IP through browser APIs, while DNS leaks expose your search queries.
