About the Author — Denys Shchur

Last updated: January 2, 2026

I’m Denys Shchur, the person behind the content on VPN World. My work sits at the intersection of VPN testing, cybersecurity, and search — I write long-form guides that are meant to be both technically sound and easy to follow.

VPN World started as a side project and turned into a structured, multi-language knowledge base. I focus on real-life scenarios: streaming, travel, remote work, and everyday privacy risks — with an emphasis on what actually works in practice, not just what looks good on marketing pages.

In parallel to VPN World, I run SmartAdvisorOnline — an English project focused on VPN advice, security best practices, and “less noise, more signal” how-to guides.

How I approach writing on VPN World

When I start a new guide, I usually look at three angles:

  • Real usage — how a tool behaves at home, in cafés, co-working spaces, hotels, and while traveling.
  • Technical underpinnings — protocols, DNS handling, kill switches, split tunneling, and practical configuration.
  • Long-term perspective — what happens after weeks and months of daily use, not just quick demo tests.

I try to keep the language direct and practical: instead of repeating vendor slogans, I’d rather answer questions like “Will this actually unblock what I need?” or “Will this break my home Wi-Fi?”.

Security, privacy, and the small details

Security on paper and security in the real world are not the same thing. In practice, it matters whether:

  • DNS requests are handled properly or quietly leaked to your ISP,
  • a VPN keeps logs that could be matched to your traffic,
  • the provider uses modern protocols and hardened infrastructure,
  • a kill switch truly blocks traffic when the tunnel drops.

That’s why many VPN World guides go deeper into geo-blocks, DNS behavior, dedicated IPs, and account security — the areas where users often get surprised.

How testing works in practice

I build tests around everyday usage patterns rather than lab conditions. That includes:

  • streaming U.S. and international services,
  • home broadband as well as congested evening Wi-Fi,
  • VPN use on phones, laptops, smart TVs, and consoles,
  • public networks, airport Wi-Fi, and hotspots,
  • mixing VPN usage with banking apps, work tools, and gaming.
