About the Author — Denys Shchur

Last updated: 25 November 2025

I’m Denys Shchur, the writer and SEO architect behind VPN World. I test VPN services, streaming platforms, and security tools, and turn those technical results into clear, practical guides for everyday users.

VPN World started as an independent “SEO + security lab”. I look at how VPNs and privacy tools behave in real-world conditions: slower home connections, public Wi-Fi, different countries, popular streaming services, and social platforms. At the same time, I focus on clarity: every guide should be useful both for a technical reader and for someone who just wants their VPN to protect them without getting in the way.

How I build content for VPN World

Most long-form guides on VPN World are based on three layers:

  • hands-on testing of speed, stability, and compatibility,
  • understanding of VPN protocols, encryption, and network setups,
  • experience with large, long-term SEO content projects.

I don’t “marry” a single provider. Instead, I describe concrete use cases: when a certain VPN is worth using, when it’s better to pick an alternative, or when the best fix is something else entirely, like changing router settings or enabling 2FA/MFA on your accounts.

Why technical details matter

Terms like VPN, encryption, DNS, logs or protocols might sound abstract, but small technical details decide whether:

  • your traffic is actually encrypted end-to-end,
  • websites detect a VPN tunnel or see “normal” traffic,
  • a streaming service blocks your IP switch or lets you keep watching,
  • a phishing attempt ends with a stolen account or is stopped by 2FA/MFA.

That’s why you’ll see terms like WireGuard, NordLynx, RAM-only servers, DNS leaks, and kill switches in my articles. I always try to bring them back to practical questions: what does this change for you if you’re just trying to browse safer, stream securely, or work remotely without exposing too much data.

How I test VPNs and security tools

My testing workflow focuses on realistic scenarios:

  • streaming sports and movies (Netflix, DAZN, BBC iPlayer, local platforms),
  • using public Wi-Fi in cafés, airports, and stations,
  • connecting to work remotely (corporate VPN, remote desktop, SSH),
  • day-to-day browsing, cloud storage, and email usage,
  • online gaming, where ping spikes hurt more than raw bandwidth limits.

Synthetic benchmarks are useful, but for me it’s more important to answer: can you watch a full game without buffering, does the VPN drop in the worst moment, and can you recommend this setup to your family without becoming “tech support” every weekend.

Transparency and affiliate links

VPN World uses affiliate links to some VPN services. If you decide to buy through one of those links, I may earn a commission at no extra cost to you. This helps keep the site independent, pay for tools, and run new tests.

But my recommendations are not sold. If a tool underperforms, I say it clearly. If a feature is overkill for most users, I explain why and suggest simpler options.
